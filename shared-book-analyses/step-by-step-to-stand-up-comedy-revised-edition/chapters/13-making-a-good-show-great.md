# Chapter 13 — Making a Good Show Great

## Header

- **Book:** *Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy, Revised Edition*
- **Chapter:** 13, “Making a Good Show Great”
- **Source range:** Page 177 to Page 190
- **Source status:** **Possibly incomplete** — the full polishing loop and conclusion are present, but Pages 178, 180, and 184 are absent; Page 188 includes a low-confidence capture.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A show improves through a recurring evidence loop: record live performance, rate audience response joke by joke, edit/rewrite/rearrange from that feedback, rehearse the revision, and perform it again.

Dean closes the manual by converting performance into revision data. Playback counters unreliable memory; A–F laugh ratings expose strong and weak jokes [Pages 177–181]. Editing removes persistent misses without destroying the archive, rewriting diagnoses structural or delivery problems, and rearranging uses B-C-A order, graphing, and hammocking to manage audience momentum and risk [Pages 181–189]. The post-office routine developed in earlier chapters is rated, cut, rewritten, resequenced into a stronger storyline, and returned to rehearsal and performance [Pages 179–190].

Essential points:

- Do not polish only from intuition before performance; audience response supplies information unavailable at the desk [Pages 177–179].
- Playback should proceed once for the whole shape and again sentence by sentence or joke by joke [Page 179].
- Rate big, medium, weak, smile-only, and silent responses A through F; repeated performances produce a better reading than one show [Pages 179–181].
- Ruthless editing is essential, but cut material should be saved because later craft or persona changes may make it usable [Pages 181–182].
- Diagnose misses for audibility, Chapter 4 revision issues, and Joke Diagram structure before abandoning them [Pages 182–185].
- Arrange a show to open strongly, place weaker/riskier work safely, and finish with the strongest material [Pages 186–189].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Polishing as a loop, not a fix

Dean begins with four guidelines: play back the show; rate the jokes; edit, rewrite, and rearrange; rehearse and perform again [Pages 177–179]. He explicitly rejects a rigid one-pass procedure. Live response changes the evidence, so polishing loops continually through development, rehearsal, performance, and revision.

### Playback and rating

Recordings preserve details the performer will not reliably remember, especially ad-libs and the audience's response to individual jokes. Dean recommends an uninterrupted first listen for overall highs and lows, followed by a granular pass with pauses after sentences or jokes [Page 179]. Video adds behavior and staging to the evidence; audio at least preserves language and laughter.

The rating scale is response-based: A for the biggest laughs, B for medium, C for the weakest laughs, D for a smile without a laugh, and F for silence [Page 179]. Dean expects a professional set to move gradually toward all-A material but acknowledges a low yield: in his estimate, perhaps one in a hundred written jokes becomes an A. The operational implication is a regular writing schedule, not discouragement [Pages 179–181].

He publishes ratings from two performances of the post-office routine. Only the “we know where you live” exchange receives A-A; some jokes vary substantially between shows, and several of Dean's favorites receive D or F [Pages 179–181]. He keeps material with at least one A, B, or C and lets audience response override attachment.

### Edit without erasing the archive

Editing removes marginal work from the show, not necessarily from the joke file. Dean calls this the most valuable comic skill because performers tend to protect jokes like children [Pages 181–182]. A shelved joke can later return when persona, delivery, knowledge, or craft changes. In the worked example, low-rated logo, monopoly, oxymoron, long-line, stamp, and Charles Manson material is removed from the active routine [Page 182].

### Rewrite diagnostically

Before cutting every miss, investigate why it failed. Confirm that the audience heard the words. Revisit Chapter 4's compression, reveal placement, shared knowledge, sound, and act-out guidance. Put the joke on the Joke Diagram and ask whether setup, target, connector, reinterpretation, second story, and punch are each doing their jobs [Pages 182–183].

Dean then rewrites retained post-office jokes. A weak “employee of the month” line becomes an audience-baited prompt; a wrong-house tag becomes a hit-man line; a breast-implant stamp joke receives a new setup after its prior introduction was cut; and a “pairs” tag becomes more explicit [Pages 183–185]. Rewriting remains trial and error, and the original can be restored if the new version performs worse.

### Rearrange for momentum and risk

Dean advises opening with the second-strongest material, placing weaker material in the middle, and closing with the strongest: B-C-A [Page 186]. A graph of laugh volume and length can make pacing visible; the desired trend generally rises, even if individual responses vary [Pages 186–188].

*Hammocking* places new, uncertain, improvised, or time-filling material between proven bits, creating a strong entry and recovery point [Page 188]. Dean additionally recommends placing sex material at the end because he says audiences tend to laugh harder at it, and delaying controversial material until rapport exists [Pages 188–189]. These are context-dependent authorial rules of thumb, not universal findings.

The revised post-office routine acquires a tighter storyline: go to buy stamps, wait in line, observe stamp ideas, notice the employee picture, complain about workers, then confront one. The strongest “know where you live” exchange closes [Pages 188–189]. The result returns to the Rehearsal Process and another nightclub test, completing the loop [Pages 189–190].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Show-polishing loop

1. Record and play back the complete show.
2. Replay in small units; rate every joke's observed response.
3. Edit persistent weak material while archiving it.
4. Rewrite promising misses by checking hearing, revision guidelines, and joke structure.
5. Rearrange for opening strength, upward momentum, risk containment, and strongest close.
6. Rehearse experientially, perform again, and repeat with new data [Pages 177–190].

### Rewrite diagnostic

Was it audible? → Is it concise? → Is the reveal late? → Is shared knowledge sufficient? → Would different sound, POV, or action help? → Does the Joke Diagram expose a weak target, connector, reinterpretation, or punch? [Pages 182–183]

## Examples and Illustrations

- **Post-office routine — Extended case:** The same material developed in Chapters 3, 5, and 8 is now tested against two performances, cut, rewritten, and reordered [Pages 179–189]. This continuity makes the book's iterative model visible.
- **Laugh graphs — Analytical aid:** Two schematic curves distinguish a show peaking in the middle from one building toward its ending [Pages 186–188]. The diagrams are qualitative, not statistical.
- **Hammocking — Practical model:** New material or riffing is buffered by known strong material so a miss does not determine the remaining show [Page 188].

## Practical Applications

- Maintain a line-by-line set document with space for response ratings from multiple shows [Pages 179–181].
- Archive cuts with their premises and prior versions instead of deleting them [Pages 181–182].
- Change one structural or delivery feature, perform it, and compare rather than rewriting indefinitely without data [Pages 182–190].
- Graph the order when a set feels uneven, and hammock experiments between reliable routines [Pages 186–188].
- Close with the strongest material supported by actual response, not merely personal preference [Pages 186–189].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- **Interpretation:** One audience provides limited evidence; the observable basis is that Dean rates two performances “to get a better reading” [Page 179], and several joke ratings change between them [Pages 179–181].
- Laughter is the primary metric in this comedy manual, but Dean does not discuss applause, tension, narrative investment, or other goals except brief smile ratings.
- The one-in-a-hundred A-joke yield, B-C-A order, sex-material closing advice, and audience decision within thirty seconds are rules of thumb in this chapter, not externally validated laws [Pages 179–188].
- Editing and rewriting overlap; the named stages clarify thinking but are not operationally isolated [Page 181].

## Connections

This final chapter closes the cumulative loop. Chapter 1 diagnoses structure; Chapters 2–4 generate and revise; Chapter 5 assembles; Chapters 6–8 embody and rehearse; Chapters 9–12 prepare and test live; Chapter 13 feeds actual response back into every earlier tool. ## Takeaways

- Record reality; do not revise from memory alone.
- Let repeated audience response challenge attachment to favorite jokes.
- Cut from the show without erasing from the archive.
- Diagnose before discarding, then test the revision.
- Arrange for momentum and repeat the loop indefinitely.
