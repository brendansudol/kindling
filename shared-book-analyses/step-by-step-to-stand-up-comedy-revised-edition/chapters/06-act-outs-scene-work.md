## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Act outs become teachable when scene work is separated into Narrator, Self, and Character points of view and performers deliberately practice shifting among them.

Dean defines three POVs, then builds a four-round exercise that isolates each role before combining them in a staged argument [Pages 84–94]. Narrator observes and addresses the audience from outside the scene; Self participates as the comedian inside the scene; Character embodies another person, animal, object, or abstraction [Pages 84–86]. The framework serves not just staging but joke generation, present-tense immediacy, character discovery, opinion expression, and sensory rehearsal [Pages 95–101].

Essential points:

- Narrator and Self are both voiced by the comic but differ by participation: Narrator reports from outside; Self experiences from inside [Page 86].
- Character POV can represent virtually anyone or anything, not merely an impersonated person [Pages 86, 98–100].
- Consistent orientation, small upper-body shifts, and “cheating out” keep multi-character scenes legible [Pages 90–92].
- Narrator sets context, speaks to the audience, and comments on the scene without turning into a detached therapist [Pages 91–93].
- POV shifts generate material because different perspectives interpret the same connector differently and activate associated details in the imagined experience [Pages 96–101].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Three positions inside and outside a scene

The chapter begins by distinguishing three perceptual positions. Narrator POV tells or observes the experience without participating. Self POV reenacts the comic's own role as if the event is happening. Character POV embodies someone or something else [Pages 84–86]. A minor wording change is not enough; the comic's relation to the experience changes. In Dean's car-accident example, “Bob asked if I was hurt” is narration, while becoming Bob and asking the question turns it into Character POV [Page 86].

The framework is stylistically neutral. Observation comics may remain mostly in Narrator, character comics may populate a scene with many Character POVs, and most performers combine all three. Dean's concern is conscious control and audience clarity, not mandating a particular ratio [Page 86].

### Four-round POV exercise

The exercise uses an argument because conflict and multiple issues sustain emotion and perspective. Dean advises choosing a real or plausible face-to-face argument with an adult one can portray, but not one so painful that the exercise becomes therapy [Pages 86–87].

**Round 1** performs only Self POV against an imagined Character. The performer establishes location, counterpart, clothing, and subject, then pauses to “listen” to the other side. The listening gaps are intended to make the imagined exchange responsive rather than a monologue [Pages 87–88].

**Round 2** physically moves into Character POV and relinquishes the Self position. The performer must discover how the other person is right from that person's perspective, using a sustainable voice, posture, and behavioral pattern to enter the role [Pages 88–90].

**Round 3** alternates Self and Character. Dean instructs performers to plant their feet, shift the upper body rather than walk, keep each role facing a stable direction, and angle both roles about forty-five degrees toward the audience. These constraints prevent wandering, slow transitions, and visual confusion [Pages 90–91]. Because the two roles can disagree about the same subject, each shift can also carry the two-interpretation structure of a joke [Page 91].

**Round 4** adds Narrator. Narrator faces and addresses the audience, sets who/what/where, then periodically pops out of the argument to move it along or make smart-aleck comments. Narrator calls the Self “I” or “me” and should intensify comedy rather than objectively solve the conflict [Pages 91–93]. The performer then reflects on discoveries from all three positions [Pages 93–94].

### Streamlining and broader applications

Once the distinctions are stable, Dean condenses the exercise into one round: Narrator establishes the scene, Self and Character enact it, and Narrator intermittently comments. Conflict and strong emotion remain useful because they supply an active through-line [Pages 94–95].

Dean then broadens the framework. A POV shift can instantiate joke structure when two characters interpret the same thing differently. In Fernando Rivera's pierced-nephew example, the nephew reads the piercings positively and the uncle negatively; “piercings” is the connector [Page 96]. This is an application of Chapter 1's model to perspective rather than wordplay.

POVs also generate material. Embodying an impound-yard employee gives the imagined character psychology, environment, and associated details that would not appear through desk writing alone [Page 97]. Moving from Narrator into Self and Character brings a past-tense report into the apparent present and can intensify the audience's experience [Pages 97–98].

Character work extends to animals, objects, body parts, and abstractions [Pages 98–100]. It can also provide distance for provocative opinions, though Dean still requires joke structure [Page 100]. Roles may be combined—physical enactment can occur while a narrating voice continues—and experimentation determines what remains intelligible [Page 101]. Finally, the chapter positions POVs as the foundation of the coming rehearsal process, where enacting all relevant roles creates what Dean calls a more “holographic” memory than memorizing words [Page 101].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Four-round progression

1. Enact Self only and listen to the imagined other.
2. Enact Character only and adopt that role's perspective, voice, and posture.
3. Alternate Self/Character with fixed feet, consistent directions, and audience-facing angles.
4. Add Narrator to set and intermittently comment on the scene [Pages 87–94].

### One-round scene template

Narrator establishes who, what, and where → Self/Character exchange develops conflict → Narrator briefly comments → return to scene → end at a natural or strong climax [Pages 94–95].

## Examples and Illustrations

- **Pierced nephew — Case:** Two POVs interpret one connector oppositely, demonstrating perspective-based joke structure [Page 96].
- **Impound Guy — Case:** Improvising the employee's worldview generates a sustained voice and new jokes [Page 97].

## Practical Applications

- Mark each existing routine for Narrator, Self, and potential Character roles.
- Keep scene geography consistent and transitions physically economical [Pages 90–92].
- Let characters speak and act rather than reporting everything they did [Pages 98–100].
- Use strong emotional conflict to fuel exploratory improvisation, but avoid material too raw for a craft exercise [Pages 86–87, 94–95].
- Record newly discovered lines and bits after improvising each perspective [Pages 91, 97].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- The four rounds are training scaffolding; Dean explicitly condenses them once the roles are understood [Pages 94–95].
- Observation-only and character-heavy styles are both legitimate; POV awareness does not prescribe style [Page 86].
- Dean broadly states that any POV shift has joke structure because it offers two interpretations [Pages 91, 96]. A shift may create the structural opportunity, but the chapter does not show that every shift reliably produces a laugh.
- Giving an offensive opinion to a character can change audience interpretation, but Dean's examples do not eliminate ethical or contextual consequences [Page 100].

## Connections

The three POVs apply Chapter 1's connector model to people and perspectives and turn Chapter 5's arranged text into performable scenes. They also become the core enactment mechanism in Chapters 7–8: the performer will rehearse the experience from all relevant POVs and retrieve material through sensory memory.

## Takeaways

- Know whether you are outside the scene, yourself inside it, or another entity inside it.
- Make shifts visually consistent and small.
- Use perspective not only to deliver jokes but to discover them.
- Move from telling what happened toward letting the audience witness a present-tense scene.
