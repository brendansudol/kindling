# Chapter 7 — Rehearsing

- **Book:** *Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy, Revised Edition*

- **Source range:** Page 103 to Page 114
- **Source status:** **Complete** — the chapter's opening, conceptual argument, and transition into the rehearsal process are present. Page 114's diagram symbols have OCR uncertainty but occur in the following chapter's process preview.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Dean argues that comedians should rehearse the sensory scenes and perspectives behind their material—not merely memorize wording—while keeping creative performance physically and temporally separate from criticism.

The chapter explains why rehearsal style transfers into performance. Context-dependent rehearsal can reproduce unwanted posture, attention, and memory cues; simultaneous self-criticism can interrupt creativity and pull attention away from the audience [Pages 103–106]. Dean therefore separates a playful, expressive *Performer* from an analytical *Critic* and assigns them different physical spaces [Pages 104–106]. He then argues that jokes originate as responses to experiences encoded as pictures, sounds, and feelings. Reenacting those scenes through POVs is meant to restore emotion, behavior, audience contact, and flexible language better than verbatim recitation [Pages 107–114].

Essential points:

- “How you rehearse is how you will perform”: body state, attention, and behavior practiced offstage can become performance habits [Pages 103–104].
- The Performer invents and expresses; the Critic evaluates and crafts. Both are needed, but simultaneous operation impairs each [Pages 104–106].
- Dean's remedy is a *Performer Space* and separate *Critic Spot*, with feedback postponed until after a rehearsal pass [Pages 105–106].
- Material should be recalled as sensory scenes—pictures, sounds, and feelings—from which the comic responds with appropriate words [Pages 107–111].
- Acting out Self, Character, and Narrator POVs builds a multi-perspective memory and invites the audience into the imagined “movie” [Pages 111–114].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Performance is interpretation, not recitation

Dean distinguishes possessing jokes from making them funny for a particular audience. He describes comedy performance as an act of interpretation responsive to the situation and claims that many persistent stage problems arise from improper rehearsal [Page 103]. Informal funniness supplies his model: a person relives events, embodies others, expresses emotion, adjusts to listeners, avoids constant self-judgment, and enjoys the interaction [Page 103].

The “Milkman Syndrome” illustrates state-dependent rehearsal. A student who practiced while seated and driving forgot his set standing onstage but recalled it when asked to mimic the truck-driving posture. Dean concludes that rehearsal should resemble the desired performance state rather than inadvertently encode a different one [Pages 103–104]. This is an anecdotal teaching case, not external proof of the chapter's broader causal claims.

### Performer and Critic

Dean personifies two functions. The Performer is intuitive, emotional, playful, exploratory, and responsible for inventing and expressing. The Critic is analytical, goal-oriented, judgmental, and responsible for improving structure and organization [Page 104]. Neither is treated as dispensable.

The problem arises when criticism interrupts performance. Dean's sample internal dialogue corrects a word, attacks the speaker, diverts attention to bills, and forces repeated restarts. Practicing this interruption, he argues, rehearses the Critic into the show. Onstage, self-evaluation consumes attention, contributes to forgetting, weakens audience contact, and then supplies more material for self-attack [Pages 104–105].

The solution is spatial separation. The performer designates a *Performer Space* for uninterrupted invention/expression and a *Critic Spot* for later evaluation. If criticism intrudes, the person physically leaves the performance position to hear it, then returns only when ready to rehearse without interruption [Pages 105–106]. Dean expects repeated spatial consistency to teach the mind that creative and critical work happen at different times.

### Experience and sensory memory

Dean next asks what a joke responds to. He calls the initiating memory, fantasy, or present stimulus an *experience* and states that the mind stores and retrieves experiences as pictures, sounds, and feelings [Pages 107–108]. Remembering an argument can recreate bodily and emotional reactions, while the abstract word “argument” does not itself recreate the scene in the same way [Page 108].

From that distinction, Dean attacks verbatim memorization. A memorized script encourages internal self-talk, takes attention from the audience, and separates performed language from the emotions and behavior that originally made the story funny [Pages 108–110]. He cites Albert Mehrabian's 55/38/7 proportions for body language, vocal tone, and words, then rhetorically adjusts the word share upward while maintaining that nonverbal expression dominates [Page 109]. These numerical and psychological claims are presented by the author; the supplied chapter does not externally evaluate their applicability to stand-up.

Dean does not say wording never matters. Some jokes require precise phrasing. His proposed alternative is to practice retrieving a rich scene and repeatedly responding to it with the desired language, just as a person can picture a residence yet reliably name it “house” [Pages 109–110].

### Scenes within jokes

Every joke, Dean argues, contains or implies scenes that could have prompted it. Personal material already has an experiential source; invented material requires the comic to construct the relevant circumstances. A bar-stool/seat-belt joke, for example, implies a drunk falling from a stool and imagining a restraint as the solution [Pages 110–111].

The performer then enacts the relevant Self, Character, and Narrator POVs. Multiple perspectives are intended to create a “holographic” memory richer than a verbal sequence [Pages 111–112]. Dean's culminating principle is that the audience follows the performer's state. When the comic inhabits sensory scenes, listeners can imagine the forest, people, sounds, and actions rather than merely observe spoken description [Pages 112–113]. His Richard Pryor recollection illustrates this: he remembered an imagined forest, not the visible bare stage, which Dean attributes to Pryor's scene-based performance [Page 113]. The chapter closes by defining stand-up as interpersonal communication that brings the audience into the comic's “movie” [Pages 113–114].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Separated rehearsal loop

1. Enter Performer Space and enact material without editing.
2. If the Critic interrupts, leave the space rather than arguing internally.
3. Enter Critic Spot after the pass and collect specific feedback.
4. Decide on changes, then return to Performer Space for another uninterrupted pass [Pages 105–106].

### Experience-based retrieval

Identify the scenes implied by a joke → picture, hear, and feel them → enact relevant POVs → practice responding with concise joke wording → keep attention available for the audience [Pages 107–113].

## Examples and Illustrations

- **Milkman Syndrome — Anecdote:** Matching the student's seated rehearsal posture restored recall and supports Dean's warning about unwanted rehearsal cues [Pages 103–104]. It cannot establish how universally the effect operates.
- **Performer/Critic dialogue — Illustration:** Shows how correction, insult, and distraction can become part of a practiced performance state [Pages 104–105].
- **Bar-stool seat belt — Exercise:** Demonstrates how to infer scenes behind material not drawn from one's life [Page 111].
- **Richard Pryor forest memory — Anecdote:** Dean's vivid memory of imagined scenery exemplifies the “audience enters your movie” effect [Page 113].

## Practical Applications

- Rehearse standing, speaking, moving, and relating to an imagined audience in the state desired onstage [Pages 103–104].
- Put creative passes and evaluation passes in physically distinct places [Pages 105–106].
- For each joke, identify what one would have had to see, hear, feel, or experience to respond with it [Pages 107–111].
- Enact every relevant POV before settling the performed version [Pages 111–112].
- Preserve necessary punch wording through repeated scene-to-language practice rather than isolated verbal repetition [Pages 109–110].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- Dean's claims that the mind remembers only pictures, sounds, and feelings and that words do not activate behavior are broad authorial claims, not externally verified in the supplied material [Pages 107–109].
- The 55/38/7 communication proportions are cited without discussion of scope or limitations; they should not be generalized beyond Dean's use here [Page 109].
- Scene rehearsal does not guarantee laughter. Dean says it avoids certain performance problems and preserves responsiveness, not that it can rescue weak material [Page 110].
- The Critic is not an enemy to remove; timing and location, not suppression, are the intended intervention [Pages 104–106].

## Takeaways

- Practice the state and relationship you want to reproduce onstage.
- Separate play from judgment without abandoning either.
- Remember what is happening in the joke, not just what the sentence says.
- Make wording a response to an inhabited scene.
