# Chapter 8 — Greg Dean's Rehearsal Process

## Header

- **Book:** *Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy, Revised Edition*
- **Chapter:** 8, “Greg Dean's Rehearsal Process”
- **Source range:** Page 114 to Page 127
- **Source status:** **Complete** — all three phases, the full worked example, cautions, evaluation loop, and exercise handoff are present. Several captures and diagram glyphs on Pages 114–120 are flagged, but the prose is readable.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Dean operationalizes experience-based rehearsal through three interactive phases: prepare the experience in a Critic Spot, enact every relevant POV in a Performer Space, then practice a concise performance prompted by the sensory memory.

The process first establishes physically separate creative and critical locations, identifies the experience implied by a joke, explores who and where, and plans the enactment [Pages 114–120]. It then rehearses Self, Character, and Narrator POVs one at a time, evaluating each from the Critic Spot [Pages 120–122]. Finally, the comic chooses a performance form, performs from remembered pictures/sounds/feelings, and evaluates whether the experience was communicated concisely without breaking joke structure [Pages 123–126]. A postal-worker joke runs through every stage.

Essential points:

- The process is interactive rather than rigidly linear; steps and POV order may be customized to the material [Page 116].
- The Critic Spot should be physically out of the performer's sightline and never placed where the imagined audience sits [Pages 116–118].
- “Identify the experience” means reconstructing the people, setting, events, conflict, and emotions that could have prompted the joke, not naming an emotion alone [Pages 118–119].
- Each POV is enacted, then evaluated from outside the experience; Narrator normally comes last because it can draw on the enacted Self and Characters [Pages 120–122].
- The main failure mode is *babble*: sensory richness can produce excessive description, so the performer must focus on the minimum details that lead to the joke [Pages 123–126].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Overview and spatial setup

Dean previews three phases—Preparation, Enact the Experience, and Practice Performing—and marks steps with square/circle symbols for Critic Spot and Performer Space [Pages 114–116]. The goal is to turn material into sensory experiences from relevant POVs, then retrieve the show like a lived story [Page 116]. Although laid out sequentially for teaching, the process invites movement backward and forward as evaluations expose missing information.

The two spaces are placed roughly ten feet apart. Performer Space faces an imagined audience; Critic Spot sits to the side and behind, out of the performance sightline [Pages 116–118]. Dean warns that putting the Critic in the audience location rehearses the feeling of being judged from front-row center [Page 117].

### Phase One: Preparation

From the Critic Spot, the comic chooses material. Dean selects a fictional exchange: after he criticizes the post office, a worker warns him to stop because postal employees know where he lives [Page 118].

The central preparation step asks what experience would have had to occur for the comic to respond with this joke. Dean imagines an unhappy employee overhearing complaints, issuing a warning, and threatening the speaker with access to his address [Page 118]. The reconstruction should be concise but concrete. “I am upset” is insufficient because emotion is a response; the relevant experience includes service, confrontation, and threat [Page 118].

Next, the comic specifies participants and place. Dean chooses a male postal worker and a long post-office line, while acknowledging that gender is an interpretive choice and another version could work [Pages 118–119]. He then plans three passes: Self listens and responds; Character embodies the employee; Narrator recounts the scene [Pages 119–120].

### Phase Two: Enact the Experience

In Performer Space, the comic first plays Self and pauses long enough to hear the imagined employee's lines. Listening is essential because it lets the experience feel interactive [Pages 120–121]. Back in Critic Spot, the performer asks whether that role was enacted in a way that naturally leads to the joke, repeating the pass if needed [Page 121].

The Character pass inhabits the postal worker's experience of overhearing, warning, listening, and threatening. It is the same scene from a distinct position, kept concise enough to preserve the eventual joke [Page 121]. Narrator then addresses the imagined audience and describes the exchange without acting the roles, adding comments if they arise [Pages 121–122]. A final phase evaluation checks whether all necessary pictures, sounds, feelings, roles, and words are present [Page 122].

Dean notes that not every joke contains all three roles and that there may be several Characters. He also offers four evaluation aids: imagination, audio, video, or another observer. Narrator often follows participant POVs because observing an already enacted scene supplies richer material [Page 120].

### Phase Three: Practice Performing

Now the comic decides how to communicate the experience: all Narrator, enacted roles, or a combination. Dean chooses a Narrator setup followed by Character/Self/Character dialogue [Pages 123–124]. The performance is then prompted by remembering the sensory scene, not by silently reading internal words. The exact phrasing may change; Dean regards natural role-based language as a benefit as long as the joke's structure remains intact [Page 124].

Evaluation asks whether the performance communicated the experience concisely and preserved the joke. Repetition is central: if the performer dislikes it, likes it, or is unsure, do it again, using the Critic Spot to identify targeted changes [Pages 124–125].

The major warning is babble. Because imagined scenes contain vast detail, the comic may describe dates, errands, motives, and dialogue far beyond what target assumption and punch require. Dean gives an intentionally bloated version to show how excess can lengthen and structurally weaken the joke [Pages 123–126]. He recommends first learning to reach the joke cleanly, then using freer exploration to discover additional material.

The closing frames the process as a way to encode experience, separate creative and critical functions, and inhabit several POVs so the audience can enter the comic's “movie.” External workbook sheets provide practice jokes and blank templates, but those pages are not in the transcript [Pages 125–127].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Three-phase process

**Preparation (Critic Spot):** designate spaces → choose material → identify the prompting experience → specify people/place/details → decide how to enact it [Pages 116–120].

**Enactment (Performer Space, alternating with evaluation):** portray Self → evaluate → portray Character(s) → evaluate → portray Narrator → evaluate completeness [Pages 120–122].

**Performance practice:** decide which POVs appear in the performed version → perform from sensory recall → evaluate concision and structure → repeat/revise [Pages 123–126].

## Examples and Illustrations

- **Postal-worker threat — Extended exercise:** A fictional joke is reconstructed, enacted from three POVs, compressed into performance, and contrasted with a babbling version [Pages 118–126]. Because the example is invented, it specifically demonstrates how to rehearse non-autobiographical material.
- **Spatial diagram — Exercise aid:** Positions Performer Space between the Critic Spot and audience while keeping criticism outside the performer's direct view [Pages 116–118].

## Practical Applications

- Create durable floor markers for both spaces and use them consistently [Pages 116–118].
- Answer “what happened?” with events and relationships, not only an emotional label [Page 118].
- Listen during imagined dialogue; do not rush through the silent counterpart [Pages 120–121].
- Video, record, imagine, or recruit an observer for evaluation, choosing the feedback medium that works [Page 120].
- Establish a concise version before opening rehearsal into exploratory improvisation [Pages 123–126].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- POV order is suggested, not mandatory. Material without Self or with several Characters requires adaptation [Page 120].
- Changed phrasing is allowed only while the key setup, target, reinterpretation, and punch relationship remains clear [Pages 124–125].
- Sensory rehearsal can generate new jokes, but the chapter prioritizes controlling babble when preparing a specific show [Pages 123–126].
- The author presents spatial separation and sensory encoding as broadly effective from his teaching practice; no external evaluation is supplied.

## Takeaways

- Prepare critically, enact creatively, then compress deliberately.
- Rehearse every relevant perspective before deciding what the audience will see.
- Let experience prompt wording, but keep structure and brevity under review.
- Treat repetition as responsive refinement, not verbatim drilling.
