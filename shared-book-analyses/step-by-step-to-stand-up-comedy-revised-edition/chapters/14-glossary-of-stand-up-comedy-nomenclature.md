# Reference Section — Glossary of Stand-Up Comedy Nomenclature

## Header

- **Book:** *Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy, Revised Edition*
- **Section:** “Glossary of Stand-Up Comedy Nomenclature”
- **Source range:** Page 190 to Page 200
- **Source status:** **Complete** — the glossary runs alphabetically from “1st Story” through “variety performers”; About the Author begins separately on Page 200.

## Chapter in Brief

**Organizing purpose:** The glossary consolidates Dean's joke-writing, POV, rehearsal, performance, and industry vocabulary into a reference system, while adding many professional terms not developed in the main chapters.

The most conceptually important entries restate the book's core architecture: first and second stories, setup/punch, target assumption, connector, reinterpretation, and the Joke Map/Mine/Prospector [Pages 190, 194, 196–199]. A second cluster formalizes scene work and rehearsal through Narrator/Self/Character POVs, Critic Spot, Performer Space, and Greg Dean's Rehearsal Process [Pages 192, 195–197]. A third cluster captures live craft—timing, riffing, tags, set lists, hurt line, graphing, hammocking, and mic technique—while the remainder describes club roles, booking practices, types of shows, and slang [Pages 191–200].

Essential points:

- The glossary confirms that Dean treats a joke as setup-driven expectation plus punch-driven reinterpretation, mediated by a connector and audience-imagined stories [Pages 190, 194, 196–199].
- The Joke Map and Joke Mine are explicitly complementary: topic-to-setups and setup-to-punch [Page 194].
- POV and rehearsal entries formalize the move from narrated text to embodied scenes remembered through sensory experience [Pages 192, 195–197].
- Performance vocabulary encodes the book's practical priorities: laughs per minute, response grading, timing, audience interaction, strong sequencing, and recovery [Pages 191–200].
- Many entries are industry reference terms—booker, feature, headliner, showcase, open mic, road work—rather than arguments developed in the book [Pages 191–200].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Joke architecture and writing system

The glossary's definitions make the book's internal dependency chain especially explicit. A *setup* contains a target assumption and misdirects the audience into an expected first story. A *punch* supplies a reinterpretation, creates a second story, and shatters the target. The *connector* is the single thing capable of the two readings [Pages 190, 192, 194, 196–199]. The glossary definition of *joke* compresses all of these elements into one statement [Page 194].

The Joke Map begins with a topic, creates a punch-premise and setup-premise, and finishes with setups. The Joke Mine begins with a setup and uses target, connector, and reinterpretation to produce a punch. Together they form the Joke Prospector [Page 194]. Entries for alternative interpretations, assumption, reveal, tags, and rule of three supply related diagnostic and expansion tools [Pages 190, 196–198].

### Scene work and rehearsal

*Act out* and *scene work* are cross-referenced as brief scenes enacted with one or more POVs [Pages 190, 197]. Narrator POV observes without participating; Self POV participates as the comedian; Character POV takes the perspective of someone or something else [Pages 192, 195, 197].

The Critic Spot and Performer Space are separately designated locations, although the glossary's Performer Space entry appears to say it is for “evaluating” the show—the same function assigned to Critic Spot—which conflicts with Chapters 7–8, where Performer Space is for enactment and Critic Spot for evaluation [Pages 192, 196]. Greg Dean's Rehearsal Process is defined as turning material into scenes, enacting POVs, and remembering pictures, sounds, and feelings so the comic tells the story rather than memorizing wording [Page 196].

### Live performance, response, and sequencing

The glossary codifies response and show-building vocabulary. A/B/C material distinguishes strongest, second-best, and weakest jokes; B-C-A describes the preferred order [Pages 190–191]. *Graphing* plots joke effectiveness, and *hammocking* places weaker material between stronger bits [Pages 193–194]. *LPMs* count laughs per minute, while a *tag* adds another punch without a new setup [Pages 194–195, 198].

Timing terms include comedy timing as tempo/rhythm developed between performer and audience, one-liner timing, tag timing, pauses, beats, and “stay in the bit” during laughter [Pages 192, 196, 198–199]. Audience interaction appears as riffing/crowd work, ad-lib, and improvisation [Pages 190, 192–194, 196]. The hurt line marks the psychological point where comedy becomes drama [Page 194].

### Career and venue reference

Numerous entries function as a compact industry lexicon: opener, middle/feature, headliner/closer, MC, booker, paid and unpaid regulars, guest sets, showcase/open-mic/traditional clubs, bringers, booking and availability, road work, and stage-time signals [Pages 191–200]. Slang such as bomb, crush, kill, crickets, flop sweat, “get the light,” and “call in a show” records how comedians describe outcomes and behavior [Pages 191–195, 199]. These terms broaden the book as a reference, but most are not independent conclusions.

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Concept index by function

- **Construct jokes:** setup, first story, target assumption, connector, reinterpretation, second story, punch, reveal [Pages 190, 192, 194, 196–199].
- **Generate material:** topic, punch-premise, setup-premise, Joke Map, Joke Mine, Joke Prospector, tags [Pages 194, 196–199].
- **Stage and rehearse:** act out/scene work, Narrator/Self/Character POVs, Critic Spot, Performer Space, Rehearsal Process [Pages 190, 192, 195–197].
- **Evaluate and arrange:** A/B/C material, B-C-A order, graphing, hammocking, LPMs [Pages 190–195].
- **Navigate live rooms:** timing, riffing, hecklers, hurt line, set list, light, mic technique, stage time [Pages 192–199].

## Practical Applications

- Use the glossary as a lookup after reading the relevant chapter, especially for structure, writing, POV, and rehearsal terms.
- When diagnosing material, trace the core chain from setup through connector to second story rather than treating entries independently.
- Distinguish framework terms developed in the book from industry slang merely listed for orientation.
- Prefer the chapter explanations when a glossary entry conflicts or compresses away an important distinction.

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- Many definitions are prescriptive within Dean's model, not neutral descriptions accepted across all comedy practice.
- Some entries carry evaluative language, and several contain typographical or grammatical errors (for example “monologue” and the Performer Space wording) [Pages 195–196].
- The glossary extends beyond what the body develops. Terms such as “aged out,” “laser beam,” and booking categories should not be inflated into major book themes simply because they appear here [Pages 190, 194–195].
- Some entries cross-reference synonyms rather than provide new substance.

## Connections

The glossary makes the book's integrated system visible in compressed form. Its strongest contribution is retrieval: a reader can move from a performance symptom (“bomb,” “motor-mouth,” “crickets”) back toward a practice tool (POVs, Rehearsal Process, graphing, hammocking) or a structural tool (target, connector, reinterpretation). It does not replace the chapters' examples and qualifications.

## Takeaways

- Learn the core structural vocabulary as a connected model, not isolated jargon.
- Use POV and rehearsal terms to translate written jokes into scenes.
- Use rating, timing, and sequencing terms to interpret actual audience feedback.
- Treat the industry vocabulary as reference orientation, not as the book's central thesis.

## Open Questions and Source Issues

- The Performer Space entry defines it as a location for “evaluating” a show [Page 196], apparently duplicating the Critic Spot and contradicting Chapters 7–8. This is likely an editorial error; the chapter explanations provide the more reliable distinction.
- Page 195 contains “monologue ... for one perso,” an apparent truncation/typo. It does not affect the book's major frameworks.
- The glossary is complete through Page 200; no claim is made for uncaptured Page 201 back matter.
