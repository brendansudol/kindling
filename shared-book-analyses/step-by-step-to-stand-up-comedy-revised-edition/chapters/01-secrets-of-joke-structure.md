## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Dean argues that jokes create surprise by first inducing an expected interpretation and then replacing it with an unexpected but compatible interpretation of the same underlying thing.

The chapter replaces the thin formula “setup plus punch” with a cognitive and structural model. The setup supplies words or actions from which an audience imagines a detailed first story; the punch supplies new information from which it imagines a different second story. The unstated content of both stories comes from assumptions. Within the first story, one *target assumption* is pivotal; a punch offers a *reinterpretation* that shatters it, while a *connector* is the single thing capable of supporting both interpretations [Pages 2, 6, 7–14]. Dean extends this framework beyond one-liners to jokes whose setup is shared knowledge, jokes whose punch is left for the audience to complete, and nonverbal comedy [Pages 14–19].

Essential points:

- A setup creates expectation, while a punch reveals surprise; Dean insists that surprise depends on a prior expectation [Page 2].
- The performed wording is not identical to the mental story: audiences construct first and second stories by adding assumptions to sparse cues [Page 6].
- The target assumption, connector, and reinterpretation are the three mechanisms that link the two stories [Pages 7–14].
- Shared knowledge can supply an unperformed setup, and a sufficiently constrained setup can allow an unperformed punch [Pages 14–17].
- Dean presents “one thing with at least two interpretations” as the common center of all comedy, humor, and jokes—an explicit and very broad authorial claim, not an externally verified conclusion [Page 19].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### From setup and punch to expectation and surprise

Dean starts by asking why people can recognize a joke even when it fails to make them laugh. His answer is that jokes have an identifiable internal structure independent of their immediate effect [Page 1]. Using “My wife just ran off with my best friend. I expected more loyalty from my dog,” he first identifies the familiar setup/punch division, then recasts it functionally: the setup creates an expectation, and the punch reveals a surprise [Pages 1–2].

That account explains what a joke does but not how. Drawing explicitly on Victor Raskin's script-based semantic theory, Dean substitutes *story* for *script* so the model can include physical and nonverbal comedy. He proposes that the setup induces a first story and the punch induces a compatible but unexpected second story [Page 2]. In the running example, the first story concerns a wife leaving with a male friend; the second concerns a wife leaving with the speaker's dog. A version that remains within the wife-centered story is, in Dean's classification, not a joke because it supplies no second story and therefore no surprise [Page 6].

### Performed language versus imagined stories

The next movement distinguishes what the comic performs from what the audience mentally supplies. A setup consists only of the words or actions used to create expectation, whereas the first story is the fuller scenario the audience imagines. The same relation holds between punch and second story: a short punch cues a more detailed unexpected scenario [Page 6]. Dean's Anthony Jeselnik example—“It wasn't serious” followed by “nobody saw me”—shows how the audience changes from assuming concern for an injured child to imagining an irresponsible driver concerned only with escaping detection [Page 6].

Assumptions make this expansion possible. Dean defines an assumption extremely broadly as anything one imagines but cannot directly perceive, then argues that people continually fill gaps in limited sensory information from past experience. That necessary gap-filling produces the expectations a joke can violate [Page 6]. The chapter therefore treats audience cognition, not merely wording, as the actual site of joke structure.

### Target assumption and reinterpretation

Dean then isolates the expected and unexpected interpretations. In jokes with performed setups, the *target assumption* is both the key assumption that gives the first story its particular meaning and the assumption directly overturned by the punch [Pages 7–9]. In the “best friend” joke, the target is that the friend is a male human. The punch does not contradict the setup's words; it reveals that “best friend” can instead mean a dog [Page 9].

The *reinterpretation* is the alternative meaning on which the second story and punch are based. It must interpret the same thing that prompted the target assumption, and its job is to make that assumption untenable [Pages 9–11]. Dean illustrates this with a grandfather who “died peacefully in his sleep”: the punch about screaming children reveals that “sleep” occurred while he was driving a bus. The surprise comes from reviewing the original cue under a newly supplied situation [Page 9].

### The connector at the center

The *connector* is the shared object of interpretation: one thing that can be understood in at least two ways. It must be in the performed setup or in knowledge the audience already possesses [Pages 11–14]. In the running example, “best friend” is the connector, “male human” the target assumption, and “dog” the reinterpretation. Dean also permits *compound jokes*, in which a setup supplies more than one connector. His Father’s Day example reinterprets both “took out” (dinner versus killing) and “father” (parent versus priest) [Page 14].

### Variations beyond conventional one-liners

Dean broadens the model through shared-knowledge jokes. Satire, parody, topical comments, and environmental comedy may omit a performed setup because the audience already possesses the relevant setup, target assumption, and connector [Pages 14–16]. Gallagher's square-wheeled bicycle works in this account because ordinary knowledge supplies the assumption that bicycle wheels are round; showing a square wheel functions as the punch [Pages 14–15].

Some jokes omit the performed punch instead. A patterned setup about a water waiter, food waiter, and “head waiter” leads the audience to complete the sexual reinterpretation itself. The rhythm constrains the likely completion even though the comic never says it [Page 16]. The chapter also stresses that connectors need not be words. Charlie Chaplin's shaking shoulders can mean sobbing or mixing a drink, and Eddie Izzard can use the cadence of a name as the repeated structure from which variations depart [Pages 17–19].

The chapter closes by making its widest claim: every form of humor contains one thing with at least two interpretations, even when the mechanism is obscured by culture, character, implication, environment, or performance style. Dean directs readers to master the model before attempting the Joke Prospector system that follows [Pages 19–20].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### The Joke Diagram

Dean's basic reconstruction is:

1. **Setup** prompts an expected **first story**.
2. The first story depends on a **target assumption**.
3. A **connector** in setup or shared knowledge supports that expected interpretation and at least one alternative.
4. The punch supplies the alternative **reinterpretation**.
5. That reinterpretation cues a compatible but unexpected **second story**, shattering the target assumption and producing surprise [Pages 6–14].

The diagram is both descriptive and diagnostic. It separates literal performance material from audience inference, and it gives the later writing method specific components to search for. Dean also allows structural variants: setup or punch may be unperformed, a connector may be nonverbal, and a compound joke may operate on several connectors [Pages 14–19].

## Examples and Illustrations

- **“Best friend” one-liner — Illustration:** The same phrase means a male human in the first story and a dog in the second, making the full mechanism visible [Pages 1–14]. Its clarity, not independent evidence, is why Dean returns to it repeatedly.
- **Gallagher's square-wheeled bicycle — Case:** Shared knowledge supplies the omitted setup that wheels are round; the physical image supplies the reinterpretation [Pages 14–15]. It demonstrates nonverbal and cross-linguistic accessibility, though the anecdote does not prove universality.
- **Chaplin's shaking shoulders — Case:** One motion first reads as crying, then as mixing a martini, showing that body language can be a connector [Page 17].
- **Eddie Izzard's name cadence — Case:** Repeated rhythm, rather than a word's dictionary meaning, connects an array of invented names; later pattern-breaking names create another level of reinterpretation [Pages 18–19].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- Dean repeatedly presents necessity claims—two stories, a connector, and two interpretations are required for *all* comedy [Pages 6, 19]. The chapter illustrates these claims richly but does not test them against counterexamples; they should be understood as the author's modeling commitments.
- Audience-created stories are not literally identical across listeners. Dean acknowledges individual variation while expecting the “general gist” to remain stable enough for a joke to work [Page 6].
- The model distinguishes structural recognizability from successful laughter: a joke can retain its structure even when a particular audience does not laugh [Page 1].
- Shared knowledge is audience-dependent. A missing common assumption can make the connector and target invisible, a practical constraint developed further in later chapters [Pages 14–16].

## Connections

This chapter is the conceptual prerequisite for the rest of the manual. Its three mechanisms become the working inputs of the Joke Mine, while its distinction between performed cues and imagined scenes later supports Dean's rehearsal method. The map's structural overview shows that the book moves cumulatively from this descriptive model to writing, arranging, rehearsing, and live adaptation.

## Takeaways

- Diagnose a joke by asking what first story the setup evokes, which assumption the punch overturns, and what single connector supports both readings.
- Preserve the difference between the joke's spoken or enacted cues and the richer stories an audience supplies.
- Do not assume the setup or punch must be verbal—or even explicitly performed.
- The point most likely to be misunderstood is the connector: in Dean's framework it is not necessarily a punning word, but any one thing capable of carrying the expected and unexpected interpretations [Pages 11, 17].
