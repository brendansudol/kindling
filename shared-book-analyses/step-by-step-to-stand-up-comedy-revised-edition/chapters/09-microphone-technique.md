# Chapter 9 — Microphone Technique

## Header

- **Book:** *Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy, Revised Edition*
- **Chapter:** 9, “Microphone Technique”
- **Source range:** Page 127 to Page 132
- **Source status:** **Complete** — the full practical checklist and concluding preparation advice are present.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central purpose:** The chapter gives beginners operational habits for handling a handheld wired microphone, stand, cord, failures, gestures, and stage transitions without distracting from the comedy or damaging equipment.

Dean's goal is invisibility: rehearse with a substitute until microphone handling becomes automatic to performer and audience [Page 127]. He organizes the advice as paired don't/do instructions covering entry, equipment failure, sound level, grip and speaking angle, cord control, gestures, yelling, stand adjustment, accidents, and exit [Pages 127–132].

Essential points:

- Take the mic from the stand at the beginning unless a bit requires both hands, and move the stand out of the performance area [Pages 127–128].
- Troubleshoot calmly—power, seated connectors, wall jack, then manager—without striking, twisting, yanking, or blaming equipment [Pages 128–129].
- Keep the mic near chin level and speak over it so the face remains visible and breath consonants do not pop directly into it [Page 129].
- Keep the cord in front, avoid stepping, wrapping, kicking, or gathering it, and gesture with the free hand [Pages 129–131].
- Acknowledge visible accidents, restore the mic and stand for the MC, and rehearse the full entrance/exit route in advance [Pages 131–132].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Make equipment part of rehearsal

Dean recommends practicing with a spoon, hairbrush, or other substitute so holding an object becomes part of the performance state [Page 127]. This extends the prior principle that desired stage behavior must be rehearsed, but here the focus is mechanical rather than psychological.

### Entry and troubleshooting

On entry, move the face aside before pulling the microphone from its clip, grip the stand below its fastener, and place it behind the performer [Pages 127–128]. If the mic fails, check the switch and push connectors into place. Do not pound the mic, twist pronged jacks, or yank the cord. If basic checks fail, notify management politely, set equipment aside, project, and continue as capably as possible [Pages 128–129]. If the performer cannot hear the mic, raise volume gradually or ask whether the audience can hear rather than shouting into a possibly live system [Page 129].

### Holding, cord, and gesture

Dean places the mic near the bottom of the chin, angled so the comic speaks over rather than directly into it. This keeps expressions visible and reduces popping/breath noise [Page 129]. The cord should hang toward the audience. The performer can look down and move it gently with a foot when needed, but repeated kicking, walking into it, stepping on it, coiling it in hand, or wrapping it around a wrist creates distraction or damage [Pages 129–130].

The mic hand should stay near the mouth; use the other hand for gestures or transfer the mic deliberately. For yelling, lower the mic and use the unamplified voice [Pages 130–131]. When adjusting the stand, loosen the fastener only slightly, stabilize the base with a foot, adjust, and tighten; do not spend time perfecting height if the stand will immediately be removed [Page 131].

### Recovering and exiting

Visible mistakes should be acknowledged and fixed because an unresolved fallen stand, loose clip, or wrapped cord will hold the audience's attention [Page 131]. On exit, return the mic to the stand at downstage center, leave it powered for the MC, and hand it directly rather than toss it if the MC is present [Page 131]. Dean closes by telling beginners to arrive early, walk entry and exit routes, and handle the actual equipment before the show—Jerry Seinfeld's “Easy on. Easy off” [Page 132].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Pre-show equipment check

- Walk the route on, the stage area, and the route off.
- Remove and replace the mic safely.
- Check switch, cable connections, stand fastener, and audible level.
- Practice moving with the cord forward and one hand free.
- Know where the stand and mic should be left for the MC [Pages 127–132].

## Practical Applications

- Include a mic substitute in every relevant rehearsal [Page 127].
- Fix or acknowledge accidents immediately, then return attention to the show [Page 131].
- Treat equipment problems as professionalism tests, not opportunities to attack the venue [Pages 128–129].
- Rehearse arrival and departure, not only material [Page 132].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- The checklist explicitly addresses a handheld wired microphone in a nightclub [Page 127]. Wireless, headset, lavalier, and other systems may require different handling.
- Dean says most hand mics are omnidirectional [Page 129]; the chapter does not distinguish microphone pickup patterns or venue-specific engineering.
- Some advice is practical etiquette and safety rather than a claim about what makes material funny.

## Connections

This compact module applies Chapter 7's state-matching principle: rehearse the physical object and movements desired onstage. It also removes avoidable distractions so the audience relationship discussed in Chapters 10–11 can remain primary.

## Takeaways

- Safe, calm, rehearsed handling should disappear into the performance.
- Keep the face visible, voice audible, cord controlled, and gesture hand free.
- Troubleshoot in a fixed order and never make equipment frustration the audience's problem.
- Practice the entire route: easy on, easy off.
