# Chapter 12 — Getting Experience

## Header

- **Book:** *Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy, Revised Edition*
- **Chapter:** 12, “Getting Experience”
- **Source range:** Page 172 to Page 176
- **Source status:** **Possibly incomplete** — the opening, venue survey, and conclusion are present, but Page 175 is absent; Page 173 contains a likely source typo.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Stage time is the indispensable teacher of comic timing and judgment, so beginners should perform now, accept unpaid and imperfect conditions, and create opportunities when conventional venues are unavailable.

Dean frames stand-up development as trial and error driven by love of making people laugh. Technique can prepare a beginner, but only performance reveals what works and builds the ability to be funny in changing conditions [Page 172]. The chapter surveys open mics, showcase spots, self-produced shows, Toastmasters, civic groups, parties, parks, streets, and dance bars, with recurring cautions about audience quality, clean material, time limits, exploitation, and initiative [Pages 173–176].

Essential points:

- Perform anywhere an actual audience can provide experience; do not wait for a mythical “right time” [Page 172].
- Expect early failure because learning comes through trial and error [Page 172].
- At a weak open mic, first assess whether enough people are paying attention to test prepared material; if not, use the stage for a candid rant rather than drawing false conclusions about jokes [Page 173].
- Be punctual and clean enough to remain bookable; observe signup, lineup, bumping, and light conventions [Pages 173–174].
- When no useful room exists, organize one or adapt public-speaking, social, street, or dance venues [Pages 174–176].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Stage time as the central learning resource

Dean opens with an uncompromising claim: classes and craft techniques cannot substitute for performing in front of people. Most learning will initially be learning what does not work, so failure is a normal cost rather than evidence to delay [Page 172]. He advises working unpaid until the comic has a reliably funny twenty-to-twenty-five-minute set, then beginning a search for paid work [Page 172]. That threshold is Dean's guidance, not an externally verified industry standard.

### Open mics and showcase clubs

Open mics offer short sets in clubs, restaurants, coffeehouses, and other rooms. Dean advises evaluating whether there is enough attentive audience to read the material. If not, a stream-of-consciousness rant can make stage time useful by practicing openness and honesty without demanding a laugh-based test [Page 173]. He warns that schedules and treatment may be unfair: beginners can appear before the crowd arrives, after it leaves, or be bumped by regulars. Sign up in advance and obey the light; going over time can cost future access [Pages 173–174].

Regular unpaid showcase spots offer consistent growth but require presence and relationship building [Page 174]. Dean recommends clean material because shock from profanity is not the same as developed joke structure, broadcast opportunities restrict blue material, and bookers may require clean sets. Staying within the assigned time protects the room and other comics [Page 174].

### Create or repurpose performance opportunities

If access is scarce, organize a comedy night in a venue with sound equipment, share logistics with other comics, and create an elevated stage if possible [Pages 174–176]. Bringer shows can supply an audience but may exploit beginners; Dean advises distinguishing a producer renting a major club from the club's actual booker and participating selectively when genuine industry exposure exists [Page 176].

Other environments can function as practice: Toastmasters speeches, charity/civic meetings, parties, parks, streets, and public spaces [Page 176]. Clean material is especially important for civic settings. Dean's own park/street history and Michael Colyar's “Guerilla Comedy” illustrate creating a public audience rather than waiting for club permission. Colyar's dance-bar arrangement also shows how comedy can solve a venue's business problem by entertaining patrons while they rest between dancing [Page 176].

The chapter ends with the linked claims that experience creates judgment, bad judgment often creates experience, and the best immediate advice is simply to perform now [Page 176].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Venue decision checklist

- Is there an attentive audience large enough to test prepared material?
- What are the signup, time, light, and bumping rules?
- Does the context require clean material?
- Is the opportunity genuine stage experience or mainly exploitation?
- If no room fits, can a show be organized or a public-speaking/social setting repurposed? [Pages 173–176]

## Examples and Illustrations

- **Open-mic rant — Exercise:** When laughter data would be meaningless, use stage time for honest stream-of-consciousness performance [Page 173].
- **Guerilla Comedy — Case:** Michael Colyar builds audiences on a city street and later in dance bars, demonstrating venue creation [Page 176]. These career anecdotes illustrate initiative but do not guarantee similar outcomes.
- **Bringer shows — Qualified case:** Some are exploitative; some may be worthwhile if a real audience or industry opportunity justifies the cost [Page 176].

## Practical Applications

- Perform before feeling fully ready, while keeping expectations appropriate to beginner status [Page 172].
- Track and obey venue procedures, especially time signals [Pages 173–174].
- Develop a clean set to widen the range of rooms available [Pages 174–176].
- Offer venues a concrete benefit—customers, longer stays, or programming—when proposing a show [Pages 174–176].
- Treat every venue as an experiment in audience, material, and delivery rather than as a guaranteed career step.

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- “Perform anywhere” is bounded by the chapter's examples of legitimate audiences and venue cooperation; it is not a license to ignore access, safety, or consent.
- Dean distinguishes poor conditions from useless conditions: a sparse room may support a rant even when it cannot validly test prepared jokes [Page 173].
- Clean-material guidance reflects the media and booking context described by the author and may be historically situated [Page 174].
- The chapter values unpaid experience but also warns about exploitative bringer structures [Pages 172, 176].

## Connections

This chapter supplies the real-world repetitions required by Chapter 10's timing loop and Chapter 11's trial-and-error approach to bombing. It also creates the performance data that Chapter 13 will record, rate, and use for revision.

## Takeaways

- Stage time is not the reward after learning; it is a central method of learning.
- Test material only where the response can mean something.
- Follow room rules and become versatile enough for clean environments.
- If access is the bottleneck, create a venue or adapt another public context.
