## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A coherent routine emerges by externalizing individual jokes, clustering them by subject, ordering them so thoughts connect, and repeatedly rewriting the result aloud into a conversational storyline.

The chapter expands from joke writing to routine construction. Dean recommends maintaining a searchable joke file, then teaches a four-step Routine Builder: place each joke on its own card or line, organize jokes into categories, arrange them so one thought leads to the next, and rewrite repeatedly [Pages 72–78]. A sustained post-office example shows that assembly is not merely ordering: weak jokes are set aside, transitions are minimized, wording becomes conversational, and a simple visit-to-the-post-office storyline is discovered to connect categories [Pages 74–84].

Essential points:

- A joke file preserves material under multiple subjects and turns accumulated writing into reusable inventory [Pages 72–73].
- Physical or digital separation makes jokes movable; categories reveal possible routine units [Pages 73–76].
- Ordering is personal, but each joke or category should make the next thought feel motivated [Pages 76–78].
- Rewriting aloud reveals awkward introductions, segues, excessive wording, and artificial language [Pages 78–80].
- A routine remains provisional: rehearsal, performance, and further rewriting continue the process [Page 84].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Preserve and retrieve material

Dean first advises a joke/gag file. A keeper should be indexed under two or three relevant subjects so it can later be retrieved for different routines [Page 72]. His suggested practice—write regularly, periodically clean the best jokes, then file them—makes accumulation a professional habit. The point is leverage: a large, searchable archive can make a future assignment partly complete before new writing begins [Pages 72–73].

### The four-step Routine Builder

The first step puts every joke on a separate index card or computer line, with inseparable tags kept together. Externalizing the material makes arrangement visible and flexible [Pages 73–74]. The second step groups related jokes into categories. Dean's post-office material becomes service, lines, postal workers, and stamps, while acknowledging that ambiguous jokes may fit several groups and that categorization has no single correct answer [Pages 74–76].

The third step orders categories and jokes so one thought leads to the next. Dean starts with general complaints about service, moves to waiting in line, uses a worker closing a window to enter the postal-worker category, and ends with stamps [Pages 76–78]. This is not formal logic; it is a plausible line of thought reflecting the comic's persona and cognitive style.

### Rewriting turns clusters into a routine

The fourth step is the most demanding. Dean instructs writers to read aloud while experimenting with wording, editing, and order [Page 78]. He offers four sub-guidelines. Introduce the routine conversationally by stating the topic, proclaiming the punch-premise, or presenting the setup-premise. Use segues sparingly. Reword material in natural speech. Search the clusters for a modest storyline that creates actions and motivations [Pages 78–80].

The post-office example is revised in public. Dean adds an angry but conversational introduction, removes a Matterhorn line that does not fit, rewrites jokes to sound less manufactured, and introduces a conversation with a postal worker to bridge into stamps [Pages 80–82]. He later finds a simple storyline: going to the post office, waiting, complaining, talking with an employee, and asking about stamps. This allows him to recover a previously miscellaneous “employee of the month” joke while leaving the weaker Matterhorn line out [Pages 82–84].

### Revision as an ongoing journey

The assembled routine is not declared finished. Dean notices two saliva-related jokes (“spit” and “lick”) and decides performance will determine which survives. His conclusion is that arrangement changes through rehearsal, performance, and repeated rewriting; routine building is a continuing journey rather than a one-time destination [Page 84].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Routine Builder

1. Put each joke on a separate card or digital line.
2. Organize jokes into related categories.
3. Arrange jokes and categories so one thought prompts the next.
4. Rewrite aloud: introduce, trim, conversationalize, add only necessary transitions, and search for a storyline [Pages 73–84].

Dean explicitly permits rearranging or skipping steps to fit the writer's own style [Page 73].

## Practical Applications

- Index each joke under multiple subjects and maintain a consistent writing/filing schedule [Pages 72–73].
- Work with movable units rather than editing a fixed block of prose.
- Read the developing routine aloud repeatedly; spoken awkwardness is evidence for rewriting [Page 78].
- Keep cut material in a miscellaneous file for later reuse [Pages 82–84].
- Test unresolved ordering or duplication questions before audiences rather than relying only on desk judgment [Page 84].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- The method accommodates jokes created by the Joke Prospector, intuition, adaptation, or contributions from others; source of generation does not dictate placement [Pages 73–74].
- Categories and order are intentionally subjective. Dean treats disagreement with his grouping as evidence that the reader is developing judgment [Pages 75–76].
- Segues are not automatically valuable; pauses or simple thought shifts may be more natural [Pages 79–80].
- A storyline is connective tissue, not an elaborate plot. Dean's example uses an ordinary visit as enough narrative structure [Pages 80–84].

## Takeaways

- Keep material searchable and movable.
- Group before ordering, then rewrite the order into natural speech.
- Let a simple sequence of actions connect categories.
- Preserve cuts and accept that performance will keep changing the routine.
