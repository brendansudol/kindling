## Structural Overview

The book is organized as a cumulative course in stand-up craft. After brief front matter, Chapters 1–4 move from a theory of joke structure to two complementary writing procedures and then to revision. Chapter 1 supplies the core vocabulary—setup and punch, first and second stories, target assumption, connector, and reinterpretation. Chapters 2 and 3 turn that model into the two halves of the Joke Prospector: the Joke Mine for moving from setup to punch and the Joke Map for moving from a personal topic to setups. Chapter 4 then offers modular techniques for tightening and extending the resulting jokes.

Chapters 5–9 shift from writing individual jokes to building and embodying a performance. The Routine Builder groups and orders jokes, while the point-of-view system converts reported material into scene work using Narrator, Self, and Character POVs. Chapters 7 and 8 are especially cumulative: the first argues that performers should remember sensory scenes rather than verbatim wording and should separate their Performer and Critic functions; the second supplies a three-phase rehearsal process built on those premises. Chapter 9 is a compact, reference-oriented checklist for microphone, stand, and cord handling.

Chapters 10–13 move into live performance and iterative career development. Peak Performance treats timing as a feedback relationship with the audience and covers preparation, openings, pacing, laughter, and club relationships. Fearless Performing addresses stage fright, memory failure, bombing, the “hurt line,” and hecklers. Getting Experience insists that stage time and trial-and-error are irreplaceable, then surveys possible venues. Making a Good Show Great closes the instructional arc with a recurring loop: record, rate, edit/rewrite/rearrange, rehearse, and perform again.

The book is therefore sequential in prerequisites but modular in later use. Readers need the early structure and writing chapters before the routine and rehearsal systems make full sense, yet the polishing, microphone, performance, fear, and venue chapters can later function as reference modules. Summaries should preserve the author's distinction between explicit systems and flexible guidelines: Dean frequently gives procedures, but repeatedly authorizes customization and treats audience response as the final practical test.

The glossary is not a new stage in the argument; it is a substantial reference layer consolidating the book's specialized vocabulary and adding industry terms. It should be summarized separately but compactly, emphasizing conceptual families and correcting any temptation to treat glossary-only industry definitions as fully developed arguments. The brief author matter and most front matter do not warrant standalone analytical summaries.
