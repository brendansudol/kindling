## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Visual humor is “written” whenever images or actions are deliberately designed to communicate a comic premise, and it follows the same patterns, audience leaps, and principles of brevity, clarity, and specificity as verbal jokes.

Kalan rejects the idea that wordlessness means absence of writing: a conceived arrangement of images, movement, or objects still communicates intentional narrative and point [Page 245]–[Page 249]. Words and images can divide setup and payoff in either direction, while purely visual jokes disrupt internalized patterns without language [Page 249]–[Page 253]. The *Calvin and Hobbes* snow-clothes sequence demonstrates how selective images, pacing, withheld information, and concrete detail make a wordless joke legible [Page 251]–[Page 253].

Slapstick requires enough apparent pain to matter but enough unreality to avoid tragedy, usually making humor inversely related to realistic bodily damage [Page 254]–[Page 255]. Duchamp’s *Fountain* and Oppenheim’s fur-covered cup extend the model to fine art: context, incongruity, shock, and audience inference can turn objects into jokes carrying points about institutions, assumptions, or bodily feeling [Page 256]–[Page 261].

**Essential points**

- Deliberate visual construction counts as writing even without words [Page 245]–[Page 249].
- Words and images may supply setup/payoff in either order or work independently [Page 249]–[Page 251].
- Every visible detail should have a clear purpose; omission preserves comprehension and pace [Page 251]–[Page 253].
- Slapstick depends on calibrated, specific, brief, visibly legible harm [Page 254]–[Page 255].
- Context can convert an ordinary object into a comic proposition [Page 256]–[Page 261].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Looking as reading, picturing as writing

Amy Hwang’s cow choosing among cowbell necklaces embodies a premise rather than recording reality. The viewer must infer the connection and may then infer points about consumer individuality or animality; Kalan labels those meanings as his reading rather than Hwang’s confirmed intent [Page 247]–[Page 249]. This distinction preserves the audience’s creative role.

### Mixed and wordless mechanisms

In a *Dr. Brown* sketch, verbal directions set up an impossibly slow walk; Harpo Marx literalizes a request for a cup of coffee with a visual payoff [Page 249]. The “This is fine” meme reverses the order, using a burning room as visual setup and calm words as payoff [Page 250]–[Page 251]. Harpo unzipping a banana needs no words because it violates an internalized action pattern [Page 251].

Watterson’s snow strip uses fifteen setup images and one bathroom reveal. Backgrounds are removed unless useful, repeated dressing/undressing beats are compressed, and details distinguish methodical preparation from frantic reversal [Page 251]–[Page 253]. Its precision shows that visual brevity is selective depiction, not merely few panels.

### Pain, objects, and context

Slapstick needs an illusion of consequence without realistic aftermath. Cartoon or bloodless harm can remain funny; lingering bodily damage becomes tragedy or nervous laughter [Page 254]–[Page 255]. Even long sequences should be built from short, clear incidents.

*Fountain* tests an art institution’s declared openness and becomes incongruous because a urinal appears in a gallery rather than a restroom [Page 256]–[Page 259]. Oppenheim’s fur-lined cup condenses a broad “cover restaurant objects in fur” idea into the briefest, clearest, most specifically disgusting object, producing a three-stage see/realize/imagine response [Page 259]–[Page 261].

## Practical Applications

- Storyboard what the audience sees, infers, and learns at each beat; withhold only the fact that creates the payoff [Page 251]–[Page 253].
- Remove visual background information that does not support setting, action, or point [Page 251]–[Page 253].
- For slapstick, calibrate consequence so it registers without producing realistic fear or grief [Page 254]–[Page 255].

## Takeaways

Visual jokes are not found images but designed acts of communication. The central craft question remains the same: what exact information lets the audience make the intended leap as quickly as possible?
