## Chapter in Brief

**Organizing purpose:** This chapter returns from story structure to local joke craft, showing how expectations can be established, violated, revised, reused, and recalled.

The rule of three creates a pattern and breaks it; a “jokoid” gives an imperfect joke a repairable place on the page; the doorbell effect defeats a strongly supported expectation; clichés can be twisted rather than merely avoided; running gags vary repetition; and callbacks give closure by referring to earlier material [Pages 129–143].

Essential points:

- Two setups define an expectation; a third item violates it [Pages 129–131].
- An unfunny placeholder is useful because revision is easier than perfect invention [Pages 133–134].
- Expectation defeat works only if both character and audience first believe the expected outcome [Pages 134–136].
- Repetition needs changed detail, stakes, speaker, or elapsed meaning—unless familiarity itself is the intended reward [Pages 139–142].
- Callback can “button” a work by making an early detail meaningful again [Pages 142–143].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Pattern and violation

The rule of three is “setup, setup, payoff,” or introduction, validation, violation. One setup does not establish a line; too many drain tension; the third should be extravagantly outside the set. Vorhaus calls the method forced and not personally preferred, but values it as a short route to usable material [Pages 129–133].

### Jokoids and revision

A jokoid resembles a joke without being funny. Its function is spatial and conceptual: it records the intended comic idea so the writer can later diagnose weak wording, detail, distance, or exaggeration. Rewriting an Amish line demonstrates how changing point of view can reduce emotional remove; changing “bikini” to “wet T-shirt” shows how sharper detail can strengthen impact [Pages 133–134]. This is Chapter 2's anti-editor principle applied directly to drafting.

### Defeating expectation

The doorbell effect names the familiar moment when a character says an event cannot happen and it immediately does. The device succeeds when the character and audience both accept the expectation before it is defeated in an unforeseen way; mechanical repetition fails when viewers anticipate the reversal [Pages 134–136].

Clichés are stale borrowed expectations, but that makes them raw material. A writer can twist a familiar phrase, relocate a familiar situation, or give a stock character an incompatible attitude. The goal is not novelty in the abstract but personal, specifically observed treatment [Pages 136–139].

### Repetition with change and callback

A running gag stays alive by changing detail, raising surrounding danger, transferring the line to another speaker, or accumulating meaning over time. Vorhaus also concedes that television audiences may enjoy exact familiarity; the strongest version meets the expected recurrence while varying the payoff [Pages 139–142].

A callback directly references an earlier joke or idea. It can close a scene or story by returning to its origin with altered context, as in the hedge essay exercise that ends by reasserting the first complaint [Pages 142–143].

## Practical Applications

Write setup/setup/payoff triples; deliberately draft five bad jokes and repair them; list expectation/defeat pairs; twist clichéd phrases and settings; redesign a repeated gag through detail, stakes, source, or time; and write a short piece whose ending recalls its opening [Pages 129–143].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- The rule of three can sound artificial; Vorhaus does not present it as a guarantee [Pages 131–133].
- Familiar recurrence can satisfy an audience even when it does not surprise, complicating the chapter's general surprise model [Pages 140–142].
- Several joke examples rely on dated or potentially offensive targets.

## Connections

Jokoids join Chapter 2's risk tolerance to Chapter 14's later rewriting. The expectation tools extend Chapter 5's tension and payoff placement; running gags and callbacks depend on the audience memory emphasized throughout the book.

## Takeaways

Do not discard a weak comic idea before isolating what it is trying to do. Once the intended pattern or expectation is visible, sharpen its violation, personalize the details, or let a later recurrence transform its meaning.
