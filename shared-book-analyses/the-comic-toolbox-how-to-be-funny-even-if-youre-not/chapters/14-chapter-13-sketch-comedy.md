## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A comic sketch should compress a strong character conflict inside a forced union, escalate it to an emotional peak, resolve who wins, and finish by changing the frame of reference.

Vorhaus's nine-step method supplies a destination for a form that tempts writers into unplanned dialogue. A beat outline lets the writer test character, opposition, escalation, stakes, closure, and final twist before scripting. Parody may imply its opponent rather than place one onstage, but still needs conflict and story [Pages 187–196].

Essential points:

- A sketch needs a strong comic character and a force of opposition [Pages 187–189].
- “Set glue” prevents the opponents from escaping before conflict develops [Page 189].
- Conflict should move from small disagreement toward emotional core, larger stakes, and extreme pressure [Pages 189–190].
- A sketch needs an outcome, not merely a fade-out [Pages 190–191].
- Changing the frame releases tension and provides a final “out” [Page 191].
- Beat outlines make structural repair easier than dialogue-first drafting [Pages 191–193].

## Detailed Walkthrough

The first three steps assemble the pressure vessel. Build a character from perspective, exaggeration, flaws, and humanity; give that character an opposite or normal foil; and force them together through a situation harder to leave than enter. Each character should want to win or escape [Pages 187–189].

Steps four through seven increase pressure. Start with a small dispute, push it toward personal vulnerability, introduce a larger risk or reward, exaggerate beyond ordinary limits, and seek the emotional peak that follows when concentrated conflict breaks the characters' composure [Pages 189–190].

Steps eight and nine provide closure. Someone, everyone, or no one may win, but the conflict must produce an outcome. Then the sketch can reveal a new frame—the marital fight is actually a car test—to reinterpret what preceded it and release stored tension [Pages 190–191].

A beat outline states each action/conflict in present-tense prose. The model couple's route argument grows into mutual divorce threats before the dealership reveal. Because the beats expose the trajectory, a writer can repair the structure before producing dialogue [Pages 191–192].

Parody can use an absent public figure, genre, or institution as the implied opponent, but a target alone is insufficient; the sketch still needs active conflict [Pages 192–193]. Extended examples then map escalating accidents and a “tire air” dispute onto the nine functions, showing the model as a diagnostic as well as a generator [Pages 193–196].

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

1. Find a strong comic character.
2. Find a force of opposition.
3. Force a union.
4. Escalate conflict.
5. Raise stakes.
6. Push limits.
7. Seek an emotional peak.
8. Find a winner.
9. Change the frame of reference. [Pages 187–191]

## Practical Applications

Draft one sentence for each of the nine functions, convert the sequence to a beat outline, and only then write dialogue. Test whether every escalation moves closer to emotional core and whether the ending resolves conflict before adding the final frame change [Pages 187–196].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- Vorhaus presents this as one useful method, not the only form of sketch writing [Page 187].
- “Ignore logic” during escalation is constrained by the consistent audience contract established in Chapter 11.
- Frame changes can become formulaic if used merely because a sketch lacks an organic ending; the chapter calls them at least a useful escape, not always the only solution [Page 191].

## Connections

The method compresses comic character, opposites, forced union, jeopardy, eyebrow escalation, and expectation defeat. Its beat-outline workflow mirrors Chapter 12's outline-first sitcom process.

## Takeaways

The sketch's energy comes from pressure, not just premise. Give incompatible people a reason they cannot leave, make the dispute increasingly personal and consequential, decide the conflict, then let the last turn reinterpret the scene.
