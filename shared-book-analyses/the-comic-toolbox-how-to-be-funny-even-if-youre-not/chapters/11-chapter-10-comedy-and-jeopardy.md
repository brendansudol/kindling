## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Comedy gains force when characters care intensely about outcomes; writers raise that investment by increasing both the price of failure and the prize for success, especially at the character's emotional core.

Vorhaus links jeopardy to tension and release. Physical danger can help, but intimate shame, loyalty, hope, or self-image may create more effective pressure. He urges bold escalation and often prefers story dynamic to strict plausibility, while the chapter's own audience-consistency rules elsewhere in the book qualify how far credibility can be stretched [Pages 145–154].

Essential points:

- Stakes convert a comic device into an emotionally charged scene [Pages 145–146].
- Price of failure asks what the character could lose; prize for success asks what the character could gain [Pages 146–152].
- Emotional consequences often matter more than physical magnitude [Pages 148–150].
- Hope and fear can coexist and intensify the same outcome [Page 152].
- Dynamic choices should evoke “I can't believe that,” not “I don't buy that” [Pages 152–154].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### From device to urgency

The opening claim is causal and absolute—greater jeopardy produces better comedy—illustrated through dangerous scenes where inappropriate responses and opposites become funnier because survival is uncertain [Pages 145–146]. Vorhaus calls escalation “raising the stakes” and divides it into loss and gain.

### Price of failure

A two-dollar roulette bet matters little; the same bet grows tense when it involves the gambler's last money, violent creditors, and a child's medical need. The exercise is deliberately extravagant, but its key refinement is emotional: an ex-spouse may threaten the hero's core more than an armed stranger [Pages 146–149]. Writers should list consequences, attach allies who depend on the protagonist, and ask what failure would hurt *this* character most.

### Prize for success

Jeopardy also arises from hope. Casey wants immortal victory; the protagonist of *Groundhog Day* desperately wants love. A mundane ATM withdrawal can matter if it permits a mortgage payment, time with a child, or a life-changing encounter [Pages 150–152]. The strongest scene joins severe fear with equally strong hope, giving the character reasons both to pursue and dread the outcome [Page 152].

### Dynamic versus logic

Vorhaus instructs writers to favor the boldest dynamic choice over story logic because a comic premise already asks for suspended disbelief. The intended response is wonder at audacity, not disdain at incoherence [Pages 152–154]. **Interpretation:** This advice works within an established comic world's tolerances; Chapter 6's rule consistency and Chapter 11's audience contract prevent it from licensing arbitrary contradiction.

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

Replace “How is this scene funny?” with: What does the character fear? What does the character hope for? How can the bad consequences worsen? How can the good consequences enlarge? Which consequence touches the emotional core? Then choose the boldest escalation the established world can sustain [Pages 146–154].

## Practical Applications

Make separate lists of feared and desired outcomes for an existing scene; add people who depend on the hero; combine external threat with intimate emotional exposure; and test whether success and failure both matter deeply [Pages 148–152].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- “More jeopardy is better” is supported by examples, not external evidence, and may conflict with audience tolerance if applied mechanically.
- The chapter explicitly treats logic as secondary, but other chapters require consistent world rules [Pages 152–154].
- Several violent examples are illustrative exaggerations rather than practical recommendations.

## Connections

Jeopardy enlarges Chapter 5's tension/release, supplies escalation for the throughline, and depends on Chapter 4's emotional core. It also previews the sketch and sitcom advice to make bad situations worse.

## Takeaways

Raise stakes in two directions. Do not merely threaten the character; make success precious, failure personally devastating, and the two possibilities present at the same time.
