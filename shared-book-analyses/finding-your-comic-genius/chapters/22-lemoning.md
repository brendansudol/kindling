# 22. Lemoning (writing)

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 22 — Lemoning (writing)  
**Source range:** Location 2942 of 4548–Location 2986 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** When an unavoidable factual or structural flaw threatens an otherwise valuable joke, acknowledge and transform the flaw into a second joke rather than hoping the audience ignores it.

Bloom calls this “lemoning.” It differs from fantasy’s invited suspension of disbelief: familiar rules and facts continue to govern even an impossible story, so an error can pull listeners out of the moment [Locations 2942–2961].

## Detailed Walkthrough

Bloom first corrects his bus joke from a ten-pack to a 20-pack of cigarettes after checking UK availability; the clean fix is preferable when available [Location 2942]. If correction would ruin the line, lemoning explicitly metabolizes the problem. His bowling example adds a tag explaining that bowling rules vary elsewhere in the universe, functioning like an actor pushing the boom mic back out of frame [Locations 2951–2961].

Rich Hall’s Scrabble song cannot turn “love” into a negative anagram, so it uses “E…V…O…L” and follows the imperfect spelling with a stronger accusation about who taught the partner to spell. The flaw becomes both premise and topper [Location 2961]. Whether such acknowledgment charms or breaks the world still depends on persona.

## Practical Applications

First research and repair the fact directly. If no clean repair exists, state or expose the inconsistency in character and turn that acknowledgment into a superior tag. If persona cannot sustain the aside, drop the line [Locations 2942–2961].

## Takeaways

An exposed imperfection need not be fatal, but denial is rarely the best response. Visible command of the flaw can create more value than pretending it is invisible.
