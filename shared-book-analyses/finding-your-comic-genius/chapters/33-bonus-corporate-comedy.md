## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Private-event success depends as much on advance coordination, room design, audience preparation, and professional contingency planning as on the material performed.

Bloom recommends waiting until club/theatre performance is comfortable, then calling the client days ahead with a 12-question checklist covering dress, company knowledge, audience expectations, introduction, relationships, restrictions, sound, lighting, and staging [Locations 4037–4071]. Bespoke lines and visible preparation can convert a merely adequate performance into a professional success [Locations 4086–4116].

## Detailed Walkthrough

Corporate conditions may restrict artistic freedom and produce dispersed tables, poor equipment, surprise audiences, and content limits [Locations 4047–4056]. The client call builds both information and a relationship that matters if later conditions become unplayable. Bloom advises clarifying the outfit, researching the company and boss, sending a performance clip, providing an exact introduction, identifying sensitive people/events, agreeing profanity/topics, and specifying compatible sound, light, mic, and stand [Location 4071].

Bespoke opening lines show commitment and enter the audience’s shared world. Preparing attendees in advance and giving the introducer usable copy prevents the disorientation of a surprise performer [Locations 4101–4173]. Knowing whether colleagues all know one another, whether children or protected topics are present, and how much the client permits should alter only the agreed private-event version—not be improvised after arrival [Locations 4186–4240].

Technical and spatial setup is part of comedy: lighting, table direction, distance, microphone choice, and a stand affect togetherness and persona. Bloom recommends explicit requirements, portable backup gear when appropriate, and phonetic name preparation for awards or handoffs [Locations 4255–4357].

If talking grows, he first lengthens a natural pause so talkers hear themselves, then focuses on loyal listeners rather than escalating volume. A calm “If you’re listening, applaud now” can acknowledge support and recapture attention [Locations 4370–4399]. In a truly unplayable room, he recommends locating the client, asking permission to stop, ending graciously, and arranging advance payment when no agent protects the fee [Locations 4411–4433].

## Practical Applications

- Reach club/theatre competence before relying on corporate work.
- Call with the 12-question checklist and document agreements.
- Write company-specific opening material and control the introduction.
- Inspect sightlines, seating, sound, lighting, and mic/stand compatibility.
- Prepare names phonetically and locate the client.
- Manage noise through silence and loyal-audience focus; obtain client consent before ending early [Locations 4071–4449].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

Bloom’s financial figures and UK business-expense comments are personal/contextual claims, not externally verified advice. His approach deliberately accepts some artistic compromise in exchange for professional reliability; performers may instead decline restricted events [Locations 4047–4086].

## Takeaways

At private events, preparation is part of the act. The highest-leverage move is the client call because it improves material, expectations, logistics, and the relationship needed if the room fails.
