# 11. Toppers (writing)

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 11 — Toppers (writing)  
**Source range:** Location 1264 of 4548–Location 1334 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Adding strong follow-up punchlines to an existing joke is the most economical way to exploit rolling energy because the set-up has already been paid for.

A topper or tag immediately follows a punchline and should ideally equal or exceed its laugh; a weaker final tag risks anticlimax [Location 1264]. Bloom’s main generation exercise is to transcribe familiar material verbatim, letting new lines occur while the writer is focused on an existing premise [Locations 1279–1307].

## Detailed Walkthrough

Bloom contrasts a rich set-up followed by several punches with repeated long set-ups yielding one modest laugh each. His concern is not an abstract laughs-per-minute quota: scenes, tension, and detail may justify time, but storytelling does not excuse avoidable waste [Locations 1264–1279; 1307].

The method emerged when the BBC required transcripts of his existing radio material. Writing down words previously known only through performance changed his attention, and toppers appeared without new set-ups. Transcription also exposed long phrasing for later compression [Locations 1279–1307]. Bloom recommends at least ten minutes of material, low pressure, repetition for newcomers, and a later editing pass. Even one usable topper can justify the exercise [Location 1290].

Topper chains may also grow through stage improvisation over months. Bloom’s own maximum of five was not produced in one sitting; existing follow-ups became platforms for new ones [Location 1290]. This preserves the book’s instinct/craft balance: the deliberate practice creates conditions for ideas but does not guarantee a fixed output.

## Practical Applications

1. Transcribe at least ten minutes of familiar material verbatim.
2. Do not force new jokes; record follow-ups when attention wanders from the known lines.
3. Repeat the exercise if the material is not yet familiar.
4. After a break, cut redundant set-up words and test each topper’s strength and order [Locations 1279–1334].

## Takeaways

Before inventing another premise, ask whether the existing premise has paid its full return. Toppers increase density while preserving the audience’s comprehension investment.
