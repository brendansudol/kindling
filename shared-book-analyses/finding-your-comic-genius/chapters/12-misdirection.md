# 12. Misdirection (writing)

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*

**Chapter:** 12 — Misdirection (writing)  
**Source range:** Location 1334 of 4548–Location 1401 of 4548

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Misdirection should be made airtight through verbal, visual, and emotional detail, and a strong twist should generate additional thoughts rather than depend on surprise alone.

Bloom applies lessons from close-up magic: imperfect misdirection may not eliminate a comedy laugh as decisively as it ruins a trick, but it still reduces impact for listeners who predict the outcome [Locations 1334–1338]. His examples show other values sometimes outranking seesaw rhythm and small plants strengthening an expected interpretation [Locations 1338–1387].

## Detailed Walkthrough

A childhood *Mr Greedy* image establishes Bloom’s standard: the reversal is memorable partly because putting aside the small cake slice produces an extra implication beyond the basic trick [Location 1334]. In his Sellotape/cat joke, the word “Sellotape” and the mimed wrapping action encourage the audience to imagine tape around a hand, while the final “cat” reverses the object and creates a second visual punch [Locations 1338–1348]. The line is not a good seesaw, but shortening the brand name would weaken the more important deception; principles must therefore be prioritized rather than applied mechanically.

The Johnny Smells joke demonstrates micro-detail. Repeating “Johnny” immediately before “Benny” reinforces the assumption that the first name remains unchanged, and the teacher’s “Great” shares emotional relief with the audience before the reversal. Both versions work, but the revised one narrows the path to prediction and heightens the contrast [Locations 1363–1386].

Bloom’s general method is audience simulation: decide what listeners should think and feel at each point, then revise language and action accordingly. Because spoken stories can be “retaken” without production cost, “it works” is not an adequate stopping rule [Locations 1386–1387].

## Practical Applications

- Write down the audience’s expected interpretation before the reveal.
- Strengthen that path with natural words, emotion, or gesture.
- Check for premature clues from a first-time listener’s perspective.
- Preserve the detail that best protects surprise, even when another principle such as seesaw rhythm must yield [Locations 1338–1387].

## Takeaways

The best twist is not merely unseen; it retrospectively makes the journey richer. Airtight misdirection coordinates expectation, feeling, and image so the reveal produces both surprise and spinoff thought.
