# 15. Persona

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 15 — Persona  
**Source range:** Location 2050 of 4548–Location 2203 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete [Location 2050].

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Material becomes fully effective when it grows from a recognizable persona; the performer makes the joke funny, and every line should reveal or reinforce who that performer is.

Bloom describes persona as the tree and material as leaves. An average in-character joke can contribute more to an act than an outstanding line that contradicts the audience’s model of the speaker [Locations 2050–2073]. Persona is expressed through energy, attitude, movement, face, gaze, and handling of the microphone; material reaffirms rather than independently creates it [Locations 2104–2131].

## Detailed Walkthrough

Bloom contrasts quotable writers without stage personas with performers whose worldview and audience relationship make even hard-to-quote work powerful. Delivery matters, but a joke about the self also requires the audience’s accumulated knowledge of that self [Locations 2063–2104]. Early emulation of heroes may provide confidence for the first gigs, but original material and experimentation must replace imitation [Locations 2073–2089].

His three-word test asks the comedian to describe persona—not content style—in three traits, such as “manic, clumsy, childlike.” “Self-deprecating” and “observational” describe material, while quietness, energy, or attitude may describe the person [Locations 2104–2119].

The “torch test” imagines material as flashes illuminating a person in darkness. Every line should expose another part of the same body; prolonged flashes elsewhere make the audience lose the emerging image. Great performers eventually make movement as well as speech pass this test [Locations 2119–2145].

Bloom then maps persona to reality level. A real-world performer can use “if” or “I was thinking” to create hypothetical fantasy without falsely claiming it happened; an unrealistic punchline may briefly exit reality if the story ends there. Continuing the impossible event requires a more fantastical persona or a reality bridge [Locations 2159–2175]. The reader should choose among real-world, hypothetical, twist-only fantasy, gradual reality-to-fantasy, and full fantasy modes with enough consistency to preserve empathy.

Milton Jones exemplifies the interaction of look, expression, and material: messy hair, unusual clothes, and an otherworldly gaze gave existing jokes a more coherent home, after which new writing grew to fit the persona. Bloom’s own move from black clothing and seriousness to color and smiles similarly exposed playfulness already present [Locations 2175–2203].

## Practical Applications

Describe the stage self in three non-genre words; ask whether each line passes the torch test; choose the act’s reality level; then exaggerate existing quirks, movement, or visual details just enough to make the person legible [Locations 2104–2145; 2175–2203].

## Takeaways

Do not search only for a “special joke.” Build the coherent person for whom the right jokes are inevitable, recognizable, and difficult for anyone else to reproduce.
