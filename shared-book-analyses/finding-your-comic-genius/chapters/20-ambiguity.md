# 20. Ambiguity (writing)

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 20 — Ambiguity (writing)  
**Source range:** Location 2676 of 4548–Location 2819 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete [Location 2676].

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A cerebral joke may demand a difficult inference, but it should guide the audience toward one intended interpretation; multiple plausible exits are a writing failure, not proof of superior cleverness.

Bloom’s “maze” model distinguishes rewarding layers from accidental ambiguity. He demonstrates how a pornography wordplay line produced incompatible interpretations, then repairs it by adding just enough perspective to close one exit [Locations 2683–2749]. The chapter extends the responsibility principle to cultural references: niche recognition may be a bonus but should not control a routine’s final payoff [Locations 2782–2819].

## Detailed Walkthrough

A Van Gogh/Walkman joke first appears logically broken—snapped headphones would not automatically be half price—but “it was on sale” would have clarified a plausible intended reading [Locations 2676–2683]. Bloom generalizes: the audience may race through a demanding maze, but the writer must design one exit. Extra retrospective layers reward success inside the intended route; ambiguity sends listeners to incompatible conclusions [Locations 2683–2693].

Bloom’s own “objectifies women / humanises objects” line seemed to refer to a screen, but a listener interpreted “objects” as items used in pornography. Mixed silence and recoil became explicable only once that second exit was identified [Locations 2693–2723]. A screen-specific repair was wordy and still cryptic; “when you watch it” instead forced a viewing perspective and made the alternate crude interpretation clear. That version performed more reliably [Locations 2723–2749].

Before blaming an audience, Bloom lists other causes: ambiguity, information overload, unfamiliar language/reference, and insufficient trust for cerebral material [Locations 2749–2782]. Demographic opinion and “comedy literacy” may vary, but he rejects equating unfamiliarity with intelligence.

Niche references can create a cozy private recognition if most listeners can still infer that a reference occurred. Bloom recommends not placing such a reference on the final punchline, supplying enough general context, and optionally signaling afterward with a glance so non-recognizers do not believe they failed the main joke [Locations 2782–2819].

## Practical Applications

For subtle material, write down every plausible referent and interpretation; ask fresh listeners what they pictured; close unintended exits with the fewest possible words; and keep optional cultural recognition away from the essential ending [Locations 2723–2819].

## Takeaways

Credit the audience with intelligence by giving them a real puzzle, not a defective map. Difficulty can be rewarding; indeterminacy that the writer failed to notice is not.
