# 30. Storytelling (writing)

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A stage story should merge what happened with authored jokes, thoughts, images, tangents, and a strong ending; real events provide “meat,” not a finished comic meal.

Bloom’s two-track model combines situation→plot→surprise with jokes, toppers, tangents, and detail, while keeping the joins invisible [Locations 3658–3681]. The chapter integrates earlier methods into hooks, anticipation, lull repair, story order, status, and present-tense performance.

## Detailed Walkthrough

Track 1 is factual movement; Track 2 is artistic input. Tangents should not arrive before the plot is established, and the strongest line ideally closes [Location 3658]. A line such as being “allergic to breaking my neck” merges skiing fact and joke, illustrating how additions can still feel like story [Location 3681]. When quoting another person’s wit, the comedian should earn ownership through scene, tension, additional lines, and purposeful status placement [Locations 3693–3708].

Hooks create immediate anticipation by revealing an outcome (“mugged in Morocco”) or a dramatic but spoiler-free condition. They work for short jokes as well as long narratives [Locations 3721–3749]. Alternatively, interaction about the topic can ease into a story without announcing it; this is useful with rowdy rooms because there is no single moment to resist [Locations 3762–3786].

To repair lulls, list thoughts about factual details, replace generic description with perspective, add sound/image/tension, and keep the main plot legible. Bloom’s office-party rewrite turns neutral scene-setting into an ironic observation [Locations 3827–3851]. His mugging story grew across 20–30 performances from two natural laughs into structured material, demonstrating both onstage evolution and the alternative of transcription/rewrite [Locations 3864–3889].

Stories should begin after enough audience energy exists, and slower poignant material may belong at the end to avoid repeated gear changes [Locations 3889–3920]. During performance, relive images and emotions rather than reciting words; shared visualization makes the account feel present [Locations 3930–3957].

## Practical Applications

Transcribe one story; label plot facts and authored additions; choose hook, spoiler-free hook, or eased entry; trim lulls and list possible details; add punchlines/toppers without hiding plot; strengthen the ending; decide status placement; then perform while re-imagining every scene [Locations 3658–3957].

## Takeaways

Events are gifts, but structure is authorship. A comic story earns its stage time by combining anticipation and truth with a perspective only this performer would add.
