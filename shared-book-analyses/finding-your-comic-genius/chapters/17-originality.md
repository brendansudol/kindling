# 17. Originality (writing)

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 17 — Originality (writing)  
**Source range:** Location 2297 of 4548–Location 2363 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Originality requires a higher filter than “does it work?”: a premise should fit the performer’s voice, resist obviousness, and not reproduce something already heard.

Bloom proposes three questions for every new or existing idea: Does it fit my voice? Could anyone have thought of it? Does it remind me of something I have heard? [Location 2320]. He treats familiar devices such as analogies as usable only when textured, exact, and sparing [Locations 2320–2333].

## Detailed Walkthrough

Bloom rejects the “only seven jokes” defense by comparing comedy’s basic patterns to the alphabet: limited components do not imply limited works. Audiences may not name derivation precisely, but he believes they recognize original performers [Location 2297]. Observational comedy receives no exemption; familiar subjects demand more astute observations and distinctive act-outs [Location 2320].

Analogies, metaphors, and similes are easy, overused, and still capable of nailing a point. Rich Hall’s extended comparison succeeded because its texture, story, and delayed revelation made listeners forget they were hearing a device. Bloom recommends avoiding route-one parallels and repeated analogy as a substitute for thought [Locations 2320–2333].

Originality is partly editorial severity. Apply the three questions to the backlog; if a line fails the voice test, adjust its status or wording before dropping it. Record experiments, transcribe promising moments immediately while energized, then apply the earlier craft passes. Failed new ideas remain generative because they provoke more ideas [Locations 2333–2360].

## Practical Applications

Use the three-question filter, set an artificial deadline, try high-potential ideas onstage, record them, and perform an immediate post-gig rewrite. Remove obvious or voice-incompatible lines as replacements emerge [Locations 2320; 2345–2360].

## Takeaways

Originality is not achieved by polishing a generic premise indefinitely. Craft and distinct thought are separate quality dimensions, and both must pass the performer’s voice test.
