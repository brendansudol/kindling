# 10. Rolling Energy

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 10 — Rolling Energy  
**Source range:** Location 1110 of 4548–Location 1264 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete [Location 1110].

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Sequence jokes according to their energy needs: self-starting “balls” can create momentum, while cerebral “cubes” should normally be placed after enough rolling energy to carry the audience through comprehension into laughter.

Bloom’s ball/cube model explains why a good cerebral line can receive inconsistent reactions without being incomprehensible. The chapter turns room energy into an editorial variable: identify cubes, precede them with one or preferably two balls, or shorten the cognitive leap if energy is not the relevant performance style [Locations 1110–1158; 1203–1231].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Classifying energy behavior

A ball generates buzz even in a flat room; a cube tends to be cerebral, unemotional, low-tension, low-anticipation, and visually weak, so it needs existing energy [Locations 1123–1134]. Bloom distinguishes “having” an attentive audience from the room’s kinetic buzz: trust alone does not guarantee laughter at a cube.

### Sequencing for reliability

Bloom compares lull→cube, cube→cube, ball→cube, and ball→ball→cube. The first sacrifices a good line, the second risks two, the third works, and the fourth is his most reliable sequence [Locations 1134–1158]. A fixed set can embed the sequence internally; a flexible performer can choose material in response to the room. The model should eventually become intuitive rather than conscious notation [Locations 1158–1173].

### Repairing a failed cube

A wordplay line contrasting British “colour” and American “color” initially failed despite increased delivery passion. Bloom diagnosed all five cube traits, then wrote a passionate passage and used the charged word “bigot” as tenting before the line. The unchanged punchline then received a large response [Locations 1173–1203]. If rolling energy had not solved it, he would have added “tries to” to shorten the conceptual leap without spelling out the answer [Locations 1203–1215].

### Performer responsibility

Bloom rejects blaming quiet rooms as a default. For high-energy styles, the performer is the power source, and stage experience is required to learn how to connect, sustain momentum, and choose the right line [Locations 1215–1246]. His two forgotten-callback anecdotes provide an extreme illustration: a nonsensical cerebral callback failed in low energy, while an unset emotional callback still drew a large laugh at the crest of trust and rolling energy [Locations 1261–1264]. These anecdotes illustrate the model but do not establish a universal causal law.

## Practical Applications

Inventory unreliable cerebral lines; test whether they match cube traits; move each after a strong line or write a bespoke ball; if the style is intentionally low-key, reduce the cognitive leap instead. Retest before abandoning the joke [Locations 1158–1215; 1231–1246].

## Takeaways

A joke’s quality and its placement are separate variables. Preserve intelligence in a line, but create the emotional and kinetic conditions under which the audience can turn understanding into laughter.
