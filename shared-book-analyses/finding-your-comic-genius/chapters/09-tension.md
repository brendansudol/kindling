# 9. Tension

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 9 — Tension  
**Source range:** Location 1002 of 4548–Location 1110 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A performer must notice tension, then remove, redirect, or deliberately hold it; ignoring discomfort allows it to consume later material, while controlled tension can enlarge the next laugh.

Bloom treats tension as both hazard and stored energy. He offers placement strategies for triggering words, “tenting” to create controlled suspense, and “running” to carry unreleased laughter into a later punchline [Locations 1016–1066; 1095].

## Detailed Walkthrough

When a heckle or uneasy moment changes the room, continuing an upbeat story as if nothing happened creates a mismatch between the performer and everyone’s attention. Bloom argues that the comedian should first convert the bad energy, because the first subsequent laugh releases both the joke’s pleasure and the accumulated discomfort [Locations 1002–1026].

“Word bingo” names an audience’s recoil at a charged word before they can process the punchline. Bloom’s two initial repairs are to place the word as the final reveal or deliberately harness the tension in the next line [Location 1026]. He criticizes both blanket avoidance and heavy-handed moral reassurance, proposing instead that a performer whose style permits it can let a small uncertainty animate a lull [Locations 1037–1051].

Bloom calls that deliberate method “tenting”: insert an ambiguous detail that feels like a match lit inside a tent, never deliver the feared offense, and pop the enlarged balloon when the audience realizes it is safe. The technique is especially potent before the audience believes it fully knows the persona [Locations 1051–1066]. He presents it as delicate and style-dependent; mishandling becomes simple word bingo.

For words tied to recent collective distress, Bloom recommends omission, allusion, punchline-only placement, or a softer substitute. If tension conflicts with the performer’s style and cannot be controlled, dropping the line is preferable [Locations 1082–1095]. His final method, “running,” deliberately declines a small laugh so its unreleased tension combines with a stronger next laugh, sometimes across two intermediary lines [Location 1095].

## Takeaways

Tension never becomes useful merely by existing; its value depends on conscious timing and persona fit. The performer’s responsibility is to conduct the room’s actual state rather than continue with the state the script expected.
