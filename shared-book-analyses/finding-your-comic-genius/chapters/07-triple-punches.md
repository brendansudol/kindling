# 7. Triple Punches (writing)

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 7 — Triple Punches (writing)  
**Source range:** Location 831 of 4548–Location 900 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** The most memorable punchlines can reward several kinds of processing at once—cerebral, emotional, and visual—and structure determines whether those rewards arrive together or dissipate.

Bloom analyzes Rich Hall’s Etch A Sketch story as a triple punch whose final words simultaneously complete a logical connection, evoke empathy, and create a strong image, with nostalgia as a possible fourth layer [Location 834–845]. A weaker rewrite proves that merely preserving the premise is not enough: premature information, weaker emotion, image displacement, and a leaking balloon reduce its impact [Location 845–857].

## Detailed Walkthrough

Rich Hall describes spending hours making his mother a Christmas present, then reveals only after she shakes it that it was a Last Supper drawing on an Etch A Sketch. “Etch A Sketch” supplies the mechanism, the image, and the emotional loss at the same instant; it also ends the joke on a distinctive sound [Location 834–845]. Bloom classifies the example as illustration of craft rather than evidence that all audiences consciously experience identical components.

In Bloom’s deliberately weaker version, the object and drawing are named before the shaking. This relocates the image, removes the emphasis on effort, signals the outcome early, and ends on an inferior word. The comparison supports the book’s recurring distinction: the funny thought supplies potential, but information order realizes or wastes it [Location 845–857].

The chapter then focuses on image persistence. If a strong image ends a joke, silence during the laugh lets the audience enjoy it; immediately introducing another image can make the two compete. Bloom extends this to set-closing order, suggesting that a strong visual line may be the better final memory [Location 857–870]. He contrasts dense stimulation with a “negative double punch,” where multiple demands create confusion rather than reward [Location 870].

## Practical Applications

For an important punchline, identify its logical connection, emotion, and image; arrange information so the strongest components culminate together; then protect a valuable final image during the laugh. Do not add layers solely for quantity if they compete or confuse [Locations 845–870].

## Takeaways

Comic density is coordinated simultaneity, not mere complexity. The writer’s task is to make several rewards converge at one clear comprehension point and then give the audience room to experience them.
