# 13. Finding Your Style of Comedy

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 13 — Finding Your Style of Comedy  
**Source range:** Location 1401 of 4548–Location 1635 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete.

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A memorable comedian develops a small, coherent set of comic styles that harmonize with persona, then controls any deliberate contrast through rarity and transitional bridges.

Bloom rejects the idea that all laughs are interchangeable. Shock, delight, recognition, surprise, imagery, and poetic language leave different impressions; a locally effective joke can still blur the larger identity of a set [Locations 1401–1413; 1480–1529]. His working pattern is two complementary main styles, with an optional third that creates controlled contrast [Locations 1413–1450].

## Detailed Walkthrough

Bloom begins by placing originality and remembered experience above raw laugh volume. He distinguishes richer responses from “knee-jerk” laughter and argues that some gentle performances remain valuable through imagery, language, and discoverable layers [Locations 1401–1413].

Dave Chappelle illustrates “clashing”: anecdotal and philosophical modes provide a stable base, then an occasional crude ending gains surprise precisely because it is not constant [Location 1413]. Bloom offers three diagnostic states—styles work together, there are too many, or the principal styles conflict—and repeatedly labels this a pattern rather than a law [Locations 1428–1440]. Sean Lock’s gradual “reality bridges” show how opinion can become fantasy without a perceptible seam; abrupt shifts require much greater control [Locations 1440–1450].

For self-diagnosis, Bloom recommends naming styles, asking a knowledgeable friend to describe the persona, testing a five-minute segment without a questionable secondary style, and rewriting or giving away lines that win a brief laugh but weaken the full set [Locations 1480–1495]. He groups laughs broadly into recognition and surprise, then lists 18 styles as prompts rather than an exhaustive taxonomy [Locations 1495–1520]. The goal is recognizable “handwriting,” not categorical purity.

Style and persona must create a coherent impression. Bloom monitors guarded front-row expressions as a practical signal that his intensity has hidden his intended vulnerability; this is personal heuristic, not a universal numerical rule [Locations 1529–1565]. A third style can supply light and shade, but changing from playful to tragic may cause a “clunk.” His emotional bridge introduces divorce through playful interaction before moving into grief, altering the mood over a few seconds [Locations 1581–1607]. A later mixed-format event demonstrates the cost of early inconsistency: an edgy line plus an atypical pun caused the audience to misclassify him before his usual playfulness arrived [Locations 1607–1620].

## Practical Applications

Name the two styles most representative of the act; test whether they and the persona reinforce one another; isolate or remove a conflicting mode for five minutes; use a third style sparingly; and build a reality or emotional bridge wherever a shift produces a visible clunk [Locations 1428–1480; 1581–1607].

## Takeaways

“Did it get a laugh?” is too small a quality test. Ask what kind of laugh, what identity the line teaches, and whether the whole room still understands who is speaking.
