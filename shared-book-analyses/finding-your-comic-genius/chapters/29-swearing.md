# 29. Swearing (writing)

## Header

**Book:** *Finding Your Comic Genius*  
**Author:** Adam Bloom  
**Chapter:** 29 — Swearing (writing)  
**Source range:** Location 3604 of 4548–Location 3652 of 4548  
**Source status:** complete [Location 3604].

## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Profanity is a persona-dependent impact resource; frequent use, especially in set-ups or early in a show, spends that resource before it can intensify an important punchline.

Bloom avoids a moral judgment about swearing. His concern is placement and scarcity: a rare word grows more powerful after the audience has learned not to expect it [Locations 3604–3616].

## Detailed Walkthrough

Frequent set-up profanity contributes little and reduces the force of later uses. Bloom recommends replacing it with rhythmically equivalent language and using delivery or tenting to carry attitude and tension [Locations 3607–3616]. Corporate rewrites sometimes proved equally effective without the original word.

For performers who swear once or twice, Bloom favors interaction early and profanity later. Early use both wastes surprise and may erect a barrier for some listeners, whereas delayed use acts like a growing grenade [Locations 3616–3631]. The high-status comedian who opens with profanity and then waits seven minutes to swear again exemplifies squandered placement; Rich Hall’s isolated word in the Scrabble song works partly because nothing has depleted it [Locations 3631–3652].

## Practical Applications

Inventory every swear word by function and position. Remove set-up uses that add neither persona nor meaning, preserve flow through replacement and intonation, and reserve rare profanity for a later high-value punchline [Locations 3607–3652].

## Takeaways

The question is not whether to swear but whether each instance earns its cost. Scarcity, expectation, and timing determine impact.
