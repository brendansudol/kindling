# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A List of Three uses two short, rhythmically ordinary items to establish a pattern, then breaks it with a much more shocking third item delivered in exactly the same casual tone; tags may extend the surprise only after the turn.

North distinguishes the general observation that comedy often comes in threes from this specific formula, where the first two items exist solely as setup [Pages 136–137]. “Brush my teeth, put on my clothes, feed the hostages” works because normal routine establishes category and rhythm before the third item violates both [Pages 136–137]. The chapter stresses deadpan continuity, brevity, a genuinely large third beat, and freedom from intervening jokes [Page 137].

Essential points:

- First and second items should be simple, parallel, and noncompetitive [Page 137].
- The third item must be more than mildly odd, and the performer must not signal awareness of its oddity [Pages 136–137].
- Additional punchlines belong after the third item and should continue surprising rather than explain it [Page 137].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Pattern and rupture

Two mundane actions teach the audience what kind of third item to expect. “Clown outfit” is rejected as too plausible because the speaker might simply be a clown; “hostages” forces a larger reclassification of the speaker [Pages 136–137]. The joke's Blind Spot is delivered through equal tone: emphasis or a knowing pause on the strange item confesses awareness and weakens the character.

## Protect the rhythm

North trims elaborate first items—teeth whitener, messages, dieting jokes—because they create their own images and disturb the short parallel cadence [Page 137]. He quotes the principle that brevity is the soul of wit and explicitly prohibits inserting another joke before the third item.

## Tags after the turn

Once the hostages reveal the hidden world, ropes and blackmail notices can continue it [Page 137]. Other examples extend a copy-machine prank into street sales and framing, or let ordinary complaints culminate in a hand inside someone's wallet. The tags work because the category has changed and each adds a further character implication.

## Worksheet

Readers choose an activity—preparing for a date or getting a child to school—write two expected steps, then invent an extreme third action and tags [Pages 137–139]. The date example's restraining-order and hidden-camera text is small but legible in the reviewed capture; its role is to model escalating post-turn tags.

# Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

`ordinary item A + parallel ordinary item B + shocking category-break C + optional escalating tags`, all delivered with one matter-of-fact rhythm [Pages 136–139].

# Takeaways

The third beat receives the laugh, but the first two earn it by teaching the wrong pattern. Spend as few words as possible on them, then make the final item force a new view of the character.
