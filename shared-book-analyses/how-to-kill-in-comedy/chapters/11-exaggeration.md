# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Because a plain exaggeration announces its direction in advance, it must travel much farther than realism suggests and remain focused on the speaker's own life, behavior, or reaction to recover surprise.

North distinguishes this formula from Caricature Artist by noting its missing built-in misdirection: “he's so dumb that…” tells the audience what kind of landing to expect [Page 90]. The solution is magnitude or an unexpected direction—using the equator as a belt, receiving a physical from a building inspector, or treating minutes of sobriety as an achievement [Page 90]. Separate worksheets apply the method to disliked and loved subjects [Pages 90–93].

Essential points:

- A plausible overstatement earns a smile; the formula needs an extreme conceptual leap [Page 90].
- Exaggeration can go positive, negative, large, small, long, short, or otherwise reverse the expected scale [Page 90].
- Keep the speaker implicated; jokes about “this guy I know” weaken persona focus [Page 90].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## The absence of hidden direction

The audience understands that an “I am overweight” setup will end in an image of largeness. A doorway problem therefore lacks enough distance from the premise; an equator belt moves into impossible world-scale imagery [Page 90]. The sobriety example surprises in the opposite direction by making the claimed interval absurdly short.

## Negative exaggerations

The first worksheet asks for a hated subject, the quality that makes it objectionable, and an “It is so bad that…” consequence [Page 90]. A controlling boss is heightened until he is apparently in the employee's bed and pajamas. The structure turns an attitude into a scene rather than stopping at a label.

## Positive exaggerations

The second worksheet mirrors the process with something loved: identify why it is great and complete “so great that…” [Pages 92–93]. Loving television leads to HBO implanted in a nasal cavity; loving a husband leads to having him stuffed. The examples demonstrate that positive feeling can still generate grotesque or possessive consequences.

# Practical Applications

Write both a positive and negative scale for the same topic, then search not only for “more” but for impossible size, duration, intimacy, embodiment, or reversal. Check that the resulting image tells the audience something about the speaker's reaction [Pages 90–93]. The multi-axis search is an **Interpretation** consistent with the examples.

# Takeaways

When the audience already knows the direction, distance must supply the surprise. Push past the first plausible exaggeration and attach the extreme image to the character's own response.
