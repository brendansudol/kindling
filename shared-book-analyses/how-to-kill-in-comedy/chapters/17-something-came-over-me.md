# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** “Something Came Over Me” frames an intentionally cruel or socially forbidden response as an involuntary lapse, using visible remorse and bewilderment to separate the character's professed good intentions from the terrible line that emerges.

North first compares the same insult with and without the apologetic frame: telling a girlfriend her belly looks fatter is merely nasty unless the speaker insists on intending reassurance before an outside impulse seized control [Pages 131–132]. Further examples target a child's school play, an elevator embarrassment, and a breakup, each contrasting the tactful response that was intended with the devastating response actually spoken [Page 132]. A worksheet asks for sensitive moments and the worst possible thing to do, while performance must show sincere bewilderment [Pages 132–133].

Essential points:

- State or embody a socially appropriate intention before the transgression [Pages 131–132].
- Attribute the unacceptable response to an unexplained force, but do so through acting rather than a mechanical phrase alone [Pages 132–133].
- The audience's own suppressed thought may help the joke, but the formula's explicit excuse is what North says frees laughter [Page 132].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Contrast creates the character split

Each example establishes a careful social demand: reassure a partner, praise a child, protect someone embarrassed, soften a breakup [Pages 131–132]. The actual response does the precise opposite. The character narrates a “slipped gear” or unknown takeover, preserving a self-image of decency while displaying the flaw.

## Apology as permission

North compares the device to Bob Saget's reported habit of apologizing before offensive nightclub jokes [Page 132]. “Something came over me” goes further by claiming loss of bodily or mental control. In the author's framework, admitting wrongness plus disavowing agency gives the audience room to enjoy taboo speech.

## Generation and performance

The workbook begins with occasions requiring extreme tact, then asks for the most embarrassing or insensitive possible response [Page 132]. The elevator example converts a fart into a feigned collapse and declaration that life is not worth living. The closing direction is performative: look bewildered as though an outside force, not a planned punchline, controlled the behavior [Page 133].

# Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

The formula's “permission” is audience- and subject-dependent; an apology can also highlight offense rather than neutralize it. The chapter treats the external-force excuse as character fiction, not a real defense for cruelty. Its examples are illustrations of contrast, not evidence that any forbidden statement will succeed.

# Takeaways

The joke requires two voices: the socially competent response the character claims to intend and the uncontrolled response revealing the comic flaw. Without believable remorse and surprise, only the insult remains.
