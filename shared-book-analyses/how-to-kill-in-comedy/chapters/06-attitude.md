# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Attitude is the recurring emotion or perspective through which a character experiences the conflict between Flaw and Blind Spot, and the correct attitude makes the character's distorted self-understanding audible even when the words themselves are neutral.

North defines attitude as the feeling that “pours out” while the character speaks—anger, sadness, cockiness, nervousness, sweetness, or another emotional home base [Page 61]. A neutral sentence about seeing family becomes loaded through sarcastic disgust; news of a boss's death becomes comic only if an insensitive secretary delivers it with casual distraction or excitement rather than grief [Page 61]. The chapter maps familiar personas to attitudes and argues that the wrong emotion can create sympathy, hostility, or incoherence rather than comedy [Page 63].

Essential points:

- Attitude changes meaning beyond the literal words [Page 61].
- It should arise from the Blind Spot: a character who believes she is sweet should sound sweet; a character who believes trivialities are emergencies should sound alarmed [Page 63].
- A performer has many real moods but should establish one character “home base” that makes the central contradiction legible [Page 63].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Emotion as interpretation

The chapter moves quickly from definition to vocal experiment. Saying “I'm seeing my family next week” first neutrally and then with disgust demonstrates that attitude supplies unstated narrative and relationship information [Page 61]. The insensitive-secretary example similarly holds the line constant while changing emotional delivery, showing that comic meaning can reside in the mismatch between event and reaction [Page 61].

## Deriving attitude from Blind Spot

North presents the selection almost as an equation. Amy Schumer's presumed wholesome self-image requires sweetness; Jerry Seinfeld's belief that minor issues matter requires gravity; Larry David's sense of unfair persecution requires defensiveness; Sheldon Cooper's presumed coolness requires self-importance [Page 63]. Rodney Dangerfield's demand for respect works with indignation rather than sadness, because sadness would seek sympathy instead of exposing entitled frustration [Page 63].

## Test by substitution

Readers are asked to picture a known comedian delivering a familiar line with the opposite emotion and notice what breaks [Page 63]. The exercise separates text from playable character logic. North then offers analogous pairings: the loser who thinks he is a ladies' man sounds suave, the desperate dater sounds confident, and the person whose life is chaotic sounds self-reliant [Page 63]. In each case, attitude performs the fantasy rather than confessing the flaw.

# Practical Applications

- Say one neutral line in several emotions and note how each changes implied meaning [Page 61].
- Identify the character's Blind Spot as a belief (“I am cool,” “I am reasonable”), then choose the emotion someone with that belief would naturally project [Page 63].
- Treat that attitude as home base rather than a ban on emotional variation; the text says the character can have one base while the person has many attitudes [Page 63].

# Takeaways

Attitude is not decorative delivery added after writing. It is the audible proof that the character believes the Blind Spot. If the emotion admits the flaw or asks the audience for pity, the contradiction collapses.
