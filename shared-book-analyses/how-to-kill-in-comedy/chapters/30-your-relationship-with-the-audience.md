# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Productive stage attention comes from entering before the microphone in the character's Attitude and Agenda, treating the crowd as strangers whose agreement the character desperately needs, while pushing career, approval, and laugh-seeking goals out of the foreground.

North separates the character's objectives—be real, converse intimately, win the Agenda—from the performer's personal desires for fame, approval, and laughs [Pages 201–202]. Because audiences interpret the walk, posture, and first look, character must begin before the first line [Pages 202–204]. Three drills—speaking in character while approaching, visualizing an emotion-triggering event, and concentrating on a person—help the performer leave self-consciousness and enter the home attitude [Pages 204–209].

Essential points:

- The character hears laughter as agreement with the Agenda, not as evaluation of comedy [Page 202].
- Vulnerability means visibly needing the audience's validation rather than appearing comfortably self-sufficient [Pages 203–204].
- Laughter in headers and setups, not only punchlines, is North's diagnostic that audiences are reacting to character [Page 204].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Character agenda versus personal agenda

Wanting success or to impress a booker is natural, but those concerns belong to the private performer, not the fictional person in the conversation [Pages 201–202]. North does not demand their elimination; he asks that the character's persuasive need become dominant enough to push them backward in attention [Page 202].

## The show begins on the approach

The audience sees the walk, microphone handling, and gaze before hearing words [Pages 202–203]. A nervous, cocky, frightened, ditzy, or excited persona must therefore move with that attitude. North treats apparent need as crucial: a character who desperately hopes objective strangers will reject family criticism creates emotional stakes [Pages 203–204]. Each performance should feel like the first attempt to tell these strangers the story.

## Character statement and diagnostic

North cites compact opening signals—pain, lack of respect, sweet niceness—that immediately display Agenda [Page 204]. If only punchlines earn laughs, he labels the performer a comic; when lead-ins, setups, and comments also earn laughter, the persona itself is active. This is his practical diagnostic, not an absolute measurement of quality.

## Drill 1: Talk to people on the way up

The performer makes brief, non-joke comments in attitude while moving through or near the audience: praise for a happy character, complaint for an angry one, worry for a nervous one [Page 206]. The purpose is internal activation, not an early laugh.

## Drill 2: Points of concentration

The performer identifies whether the home attitude is positive or negative, then vividly recalls a real incident that evokes it [Pages 206–208]. North imagines an uncredited hockey goal to generate annoyance; other examples use honor, fleas, intoxication, police danger, or an athletic win. The worksheet tests several memories against actual material and retains whichever produces the exact emotional state [Pages 207–208].

## Drill 3: People as triggers

A person can provide rhythm or energy: North reports Johnny Carson picturing Jack Benny to slow his pacing and George Carlin picturing a ten-year-old for amazement and energy [Page 209]. All three drills redirect attention away from self-monitoring and toward a playable stimulus.

# Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

`before-show trigger → home Attitude → visible walk/approach → character statement → Agenda-driven conversation → laughter interpreted as agreement` [Pages 202–209].

# Takeaways

Do not wait for the first line to become the character. Choose a repeatable sensory or social trigger, enter the room already needing what the character needs, and let that need organize the relationship.
