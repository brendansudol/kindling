# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** “I Knew It Was Time” reveals a character's extreme delay in recognizing an obvious problem by naming the sensible decision only after a final circumstance has become impossibly bad—or occasionally by reversing that order.

North's opening examples discover the need for divorce after a spouse dates Earl, the need for a new job after a desk moves to an alley, and the need for drinking help after waking in a furniture store [Page 171]. The writer begins by imagining the worst possible situation, then states what even “any idiot” would finally realize; the surprising fact is usually withheld to the end [Pages 171–172]. The worksheet pairs catastrophic “When” conditions with understated “Time to” conclusions [Page 172].

Essential points:

- The recognition arrives much later than a reasonable person's would, creating Blind Spot [Page 171].
- The formula is efficient enough for a short joke but can open a larger topic [Page 171].
- Reveal order is flexible: normally hide the extreme fact, but some pairings improve when the situation comes first [Page 171].

# Detailed Walkthrough

The mechanism joins scale mismatch and delayed self-awareness. A person surrounded by federal agents and tear gas merely decides it is time to quit a “little” drug-selling hobby; someone bound and threatened with a blowtorch recalls forgetting a safe word [Page 172]. Catastrophe is grammatically subordinated to a modest life adjustment.

The workbook asks writers to take love, work, adventure, or any other topic, create an out-of-control endpoint, and pair it with the belated decision [Page 172]. The strongest form preserves the extreme detail until the last possible moment, though North notes that stating the situation before the conclusion can itself heighten understatement [Page 171].

# Takeaways

Write the warning sign far beyond the point of reasonable recognition, then let the character describe the resulting decision as calm, ordinary self-improvement.
