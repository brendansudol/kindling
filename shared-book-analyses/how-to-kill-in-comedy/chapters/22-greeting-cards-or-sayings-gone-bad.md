# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Complete a familiar positive saying or greeting-card opening with a surprising character-specific negative ending, using recognition as built-in misdirection and a serious header to make the line part of an act rather than an isolated gag.

Common beginnings—“Absence makes the heart grow fonder,” “If at first you don't succeed,” or “If you love something, let it go”—cause the audience to mentally supply the conventional ending before North replaces it with rejection, bribery, paternity, or divorce [Page 154]. He imposes two conditions: the new finish must reflect the established Flaw and Agenda, and the saying should be introduced by a header that creates sincere context [Page 155]. The format can also close a set as purported life wisdom [Page 155].

Essential points:

- Familiarity performs the setup; the writer's primary task is a genuinely surprising new finish [Pages 154–155].
- Tone and subject must fit the persona rather than display arbitrary meanness [Page 155].
- A header establishes why this character is quoting the phrase now [Page 155].

# Detailed Walkthrough

The formula relies on audience completion. Hearing “It is better to have loved and lost…” starts a stored phrase, so “as long as you get the house” switches expected sentiment into material self-interest [Page 154]. Most source phrases are positive, making a negative ending a convenient contrast, though negativity is a tendency rather than a formal requirement.

North then returns to character. A flighty persona should not suddenly supply a harsh line simply because it is a workable joke; the finish should be what that persona's Agenda and Flaw would naturally add [Page 155]. A short introductory line about exchanging cards with an ex makes “Wishing you were here…” into a motivated statement, and a set-closing “something I live by” header enlarges the stakes before the corrupted maxim [Page 155].

# Practical Applications

List familiar phrase openings without their endings. For each, write several completions from the character's central desire or flaw, then add a one-sentence header that sincerely justifies quoting it [Page 155].

# Takeaways

Recognition creates cheap setup but not a complete performance. Character fit and a motivated header turn the altered saying from a detachable one-liner into persona material.
