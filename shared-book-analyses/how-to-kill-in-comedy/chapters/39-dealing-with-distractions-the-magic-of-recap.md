# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** When a visible event captures the room, accurately recap it before continuing, then add one character-specific comment; shared acknowledgment clears audience attention more reliably than ignoring the event or improvising a detachable joke.

North lists departures, sneezes, sirens, broken glass, new arrivals, unusual audience composition, and drink announcements as distractions that become the room's current truth [Pages 257–258]. Recap repeats what everyone saw, demonstrating shared presence and releasing the audience from processing it [Page 258]. Only then does the character interpret the event through Agenda and return to prepared material; the closing cautions require factual accuracy, no forced joke, and patient detail [Pages 258–259].

Essential points:

- Apparent material failure after a distraction may be an attention failure [Pages 257–258].
- Report first, interpret second, resume third [Pages 258–259].
- Character comments vary: departure confirms romantic rejection, broken glasses confirm surrounding disaster, new arrivals confirm popularity [Page 258].
- A serious event can be acknowledged without denying its reality; the example uses medical removal to transition into uncertainty rather than mock the patient [Page 258].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Why ignoring does not work

The audience may hear words while mentally replaying the tray crash or watching people move [Pages 257–258]. Going ahead creates blank stares that the performer may misdiagnose as a bad joke. The analogy is asking about movies immediately after a friend spills a drink.

## Recap as shared reality

The first task is a plain description: half the room left, a waiter dropped glasses, beers are discounted [Page 258]. This confirms that performer and audience occupy the same event. The character then appends an Agenda-consistent reading—poor love life, inevitable disaster, confused thinking—and continues.

## Accuracy and restraint

North warns against distorting what the audience witnessed; spin creates friction rather than release [Page 259]. He also rejects instant wordplay such as a jewelry-shop line after dropped glass because it may earn a small laugh yet weaken Agenda. Slow, complete reporting is itself the reset; the character response need not be a formal joke [Page 259].

# Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

`distraction → pause → accurate neutral recap → one Agenda/Attitude comment → prepared material` [Pages 257–259].

# Takeaways

Recap is attention repair. Its power comes from proving that the character is present in the same room, not from displaying improvisational cleverness.
