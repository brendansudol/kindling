# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** If an emcee places an unexpected description in the room, acknowledge it with an in-character agreement or disagreement before the first prepared bit; do not gamble the opening on an improvised joke or let the emcee explain the persona.

North estimates that most introductions add material beyond a name—friendship claims, appearance comments, praise, or odd backstory—that the audience continues thinking about when the act arrives [Pages 242–243]. Ignoring it causes divided attention, but a witty comeback is risky before the audience knows the character [Page 243]. The safer method is to compare the introduction with the Agenda and Blind Spot, make a non-demanding character comment, and flow into the proven opener [Pages 243–247].

Essential points:

- The audience needs closure on whatever the emcee just made salient [Pages 242–243].
- A comment can establish Attitude without requiring a laugh; an improvised joke demands one [Pages 243–244].
- React from the Blind Spot—what the character believes—not from the Flaw the audience will later discover [Pages 244–247].
- Give the emcee only name and relevant credits; do not supply a joke or explanation of why the act is funny [Pages 244–248].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Toxic residue and attention

An emcee may forget a supposed friend's name, label an act confused, or praise excitement [Pages 242–243]. These can be positive or negative, but either becomes the “truth of the room.” A silent transition into prepared material leaves the audience awaiting a response.

## The failed witty opening

North recounts a Playboy Club audition where he improvised an analogy about a ceiling dome after being introduced as new [Page 243]. The audience did not yet know the team's characters, the line failed, and the audition collapsed. The anecdote supports his risk argument: a mid-set line can rely on established persona, while an opening quip cannot.

## Agree or disagree in Agenda

A self-loving character can celebrate instant friendship with an unknown emcee; a sarcastic character can treat that relationship as the best available; a paranoid one can suspect undercover surveillance [Pages 243–244]. A spacey character called “out of it” should deny the label because the Blind Spot insists on togetherness [Page 244]. The comment resolves the introduction and sets up the first character-consistent bit.

## Limit what the emcee knows

North warns against giving an emcee a setup, character line, or explanation [Pages 244–247]. Added touches, poor explanation, or even using the comedian's joke can preempt discovery. Name and legitimate credits are enough; the performer should reveal the character.

# Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

`hear introduction → compare with Agenda/Blind Spot → if aligned, agree in character; if conflicting, deny in character → transition to prepared opener` [Pages 244–247].

# Takeaways

Resolve the introduction without turning it into an improvisation contest. A short comment protects attention and teaches the audience how the character interprets the room.
