# Chapter in Brief

**Organizing purpose:** The chapter presents rehearsal drills that increase urgency, dramatic presence, clarity, and emotional scale so memorized material reaches the audience as a live conversation rather than a recited monologue.

North first changes the imagined listener: speak to a parent one desperately wants to please or to a judge who may impose a sentence, producing approval-seeking urgency [Pages 224–225]. “Ghost Story” uses pauses, vocal contrast, present tense, word removal, and a physical “curtain raise” to place listeners inside a scene [Pages 225–227]. Speaking as clearly as one would to eight-year-olds creates command and phrase-by-phrase checking, while “Emote!” deliberately overshoots emotion to counter habitual underplaying [Pages 227–229].

Essential points:

- Imagined stakes can activate Agenda without changing the words [Pages 224–225].
- Storytelling technique requires compression: dramatic pauses work only when superfluous wording is removed [Pages 225–226].
- Clear, child-directed communication is not baby talk; it is deliberate enunciation, checking, and fear reduction [Pages 227–228].
- What feels over-the-top internally may appear merely engaged externally [Pages 228–229].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Parents and the Judge

Addressing a desired parent enlarges “listen to this,” sympathy, and approval; addressing a judge creates pleading desperation [Pages 224–225]. Both drills make Agenda urgent and keep the performer from treating the act as a monologue.

## Ghost Story

The performer leans in, slows, pauses, varies volume, and builds toward a reveal as though scaring a child [Page 225]. A pants-and-petition joke demonstrates tension and release. North then adds “raise the curtain”: change body and hands, shift from past to present, and let the audience visualize the scene happening now [Pages 225–226]. Four explicit tools define the exercise—pauses, vocal lows/highs, deletion of excess words, and embodied present-tense entry [Pages 225–227]. It is best suited to purportedly lived anecdotes and need not govern every bit.

## Talk as if to eight-year-olds

Eight is chosen as old enough to understand but young enough not to intimidate [Pages 227–228]. The performer enunciates, divides thoughts into phrases, asks “right?” or “do you know what I mean?”, and looks for acknowledgement. North says this slows delivery, creates conversational turns, establishes command, and reduces fear of judgment [Page 227]. A partner can report whether the result felt patronizing or simply intense [Page 228].

## Emote beyond comfort

Repetition, vulnerability avoidance, fear of alienation, and fear of seeming excessive all reduce visible emotion [Pages 228–229]. The drill performs a familiar piece with soap-opera-scale emotion that still matches the correct Attitude, then asks a partner whether it actually seemed unreal. North expects many performers to discover that the larger version feels more genuine.

# Practical Applications

Apply one drill at a time to a well-known bit and record the difference in pauses, gaze, volume, and felt stakes. Ghost Story should be reserved for narrative material; parent/judge and child-listener drills are broader attention tools [Pages 224–229].

# Takeaways

Connection is trained by changing whom the performer imagines addressing and what is at stake. Technique should make the fiction feel present, not merely decorate delivery.
