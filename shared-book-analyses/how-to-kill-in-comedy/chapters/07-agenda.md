# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Agenda is the character's urgent reason for addressing strangers—the belief or self-image he or she wants the audience to validate—and it unifies material, transitions, openings, and live reactions while concealing the performer's actual goal of getting laughs.

North distinguishes the actor's practical objective from the character's objective: the performer wants laughter, but the character must sincerely try to prove something vital, such as being nice, reasonable, mistreated, or alarmed for good reason [Pages 66–67]. Because punchlines repeatedly reveal the opposite, Agenda becomes sustained misdirection and a source of comic failure [Pages 67–69]. It also supplies an emotional through-line that can guide transitions, greetings, auditions, heckler responses, and preparation across changing real-world moods [Pages 67–69].

Essential points:

- Agenda answers why this person is speaking to this audience now [Page 66].
- It is cohesive without requiring an explicit catchphrase; after repetition, it becomes silent subtext [Page 67].
- Great comic characters never finally achieve their agenda, because each attempt at vindication exposes the flaw again [Page 69].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## A motive other than laughter

North warns that an apparent desire to “get laughs” leaves the speaker visibly waiting for punchline judgment. A character agenda instead makes the act feel like a consequential conversation: Amy Schumer wants to demonstrate niceness, Ricky Gervais friendliness, and Lewis Black the real seriousness of his grievances [Pages 66–67]. The audience can then laugh at a person pursuing a need rather than grade a sequence of jokes.

## The master plan as misdirection

Agenda unifies the act because every topic becomes another attempt to prove the same underlying claim. Roseanne Barr's domestic-goddess campaign reveals the reverse; Dangerfield's demand for respect demonstrates why he does not merit it; Jeselnik's charm offensive reveals cruelty [Page 67]. The persuasive effort is therefore structurally doomed but generative.

## Cohesion without monotony

The chapter anticipates the objection that a repeating agenda makes a character one-note. North answers that a real, flexible theme can express itself unpredictably across thousands of situations [Page 67]. Once established, the agenda need not be spoken. It creates “emotional transitions” between topics—an implied “here is another way the world is failing” or “another proof I am cool”—instead of mechanical logical segues such as “speaking of airports” [Page 67].

## Performance preparation

Because live performers arrive in different moods, recalling the agenda puts them into the character's necessary emotional zone before an opening line [Page 68]. Agenda also keeps the character from joining the audience's laughter: the actor knows the scene is funny, but the character plays the persuasive objective straight [Page 68].

## The imaginary backstory

North proposes picturing family and friends repeatedly accusing the fictional character of the central flaw. The character then imagines that objective strangers will finally vindicate the opposite belief [Page 69]. The result is comic dramatic irony: the audience offers laughter, not approval. North's doorway image—repeatedly walking into the wall beside the opening—captures the endless failure [Pages 69–70].

# Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

**Agenda construction:** imagine the criticism the character rejects → formulate the opposite claim the character needs strangers to validate → treat each bit, transition, and reaction as another attempt to prove it [Pages 69–70].

# Connections

Agenda completes the four-part character: Flaw is the visible problem, Blind Spot supplies the false self-belief, Attitude expresses that belief emotionally, and Agenda turns it outward as persuasion. It also anticipates Part Three, because North explicitly says Agenda guides introductions, transitions, hecklers, interviews, and auditions [Pages 67–68].

# Takeaways

Before performing, ask what the character wants from these particular strangers—other than laughter. A strong answer creates stakes and coherence. The defining comic result is failed persuasion: the character keeps proving the opposite of the claim.
