# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Comedic Revenge takes a recurring behavior that irritates the speaker and transfers its exact language or logic into a new situation where the original offender suffers the same frustration.

The formula begins with a recognizable repeated annoyance, such as a cable company's eight-hour appointment window or a coworker's romantic rejection [Pages 122–123]. The writer then switches situations and repurposes the offender's own excuses: the cable employee's date receives the same vague pickup window; the rejecting coworker is told that watching her desk would create unwanted intimacy [Pages 122–123]. The worksheets move from a list of annoyances to candidate situations and encourage sustained pieces with multiple punchlines [Pages 123–125].

Essential points:

- The source behavior must be recognizable or explicitly explained [Page 123].
- Revenge preserves the offender's pattern while changing who experiences it and where [Pages 122–123].
- Like Combination, a long revenge run should not be interrupted by unrelated formula beats [Page 125].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Identify the repeated script

North recommends institutions or people who do the same thing repeatedly: a relative urging employment, a parent demanding competition, or a company imposing unreasonable scheduling [Pages 122–123]. Recurrence supplies a compact script the audience can recognize and later enjoy seeing reversed.

## Transfer, do not merely retaliate

The office example does not answer rejection with generic insult; it applies romantic-rejection language to a mundane request to watch a desk [Page 122]. Similarly, a loud driver whose car is lured into the woods asks for help but is told that his own music prevents the speaker from hearing [Page 123]. **Illustration:** the examples show formulaic symmetry, not endorsement of the depicted harm.

## Workbook sequence

Readers list behaviors that bug them, give each a short label, and brainstorm new situations such as a date, medical appointment, wedding, bathroom, or sex [Page 123]. The task is then to map original phrases or actions into the new context. North notes that a strong pairing can support several punchlines, provided the piece maintains its revenge logic [Page 125].

# Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

The examples sometimes escalate annoyance into extreme physical consequences. Within the chapter these are comic fictions, not evidence or practical advice. Audience sympathy and perceived proportionality may affect whether the reversal feels satisfying or merely hostile, a variable the text does not develop.

# Takeaways

The formula's engine is poetic symmetry. Preserve the offender's wording and behavior as precisely as possible, then make a changed context expose its absurdity.
