# Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Mistaken Who/Where turns Combination into a scene by having a character act according to one person or place while actually interacting with another, generating successive beats from how statements valid in the assumed world acquire different meanings in the real one.

North offers two modes. A Mistaken Who scene may openly explain that the speaker called a mother instead of a girlfriend or a father instead of a therapist, then extend the collision through dialogue [Pages 163–164]. Mistaken Where may either establish the error and sustain it or conceal the actual place for a fast final reveal, as a medical examination turns out to occur at the dentist [Page 164]. The worksheets prioritize finding productive pairs and writing both sides of the exchange before solving the justification [Pages 164–170].

Essential points:

- Clearly establish the two worlds, then keep every beat inside their collision [Pages 163–165].
- Write punchlines before the excuse for the mistake; drunkenness, distraction, or a wrong phone button can justify a productive pairing later [Page 165].
- The piece can be a long escalating dialogue or a short concealed reveal [Page 164].

# Detailed Walkthrough

## Mistaken Who

The girlfriend/mother call uses pictures, whipped cream, pickles, and laundry line so each speaker hears a different relationship script; the final consequence is a mother who stops taking calls [Pages 163–164]. The therapist/father version lets confessions about parental blame and violent fantasy reach the accused parent directly [Page 164]. In both, each response must remain plausible for the person actually present while being misread by the speaker.

## Mistaken Where

A patient undresses, climbs into a chair, and discusses discomfort before learning the “doctor” is a dentist [Page 164]. A meat-shop order placed at a morgue produces legal consequences. These can work as late Switches because the setup withholds the location, or as extended Combinations when both places are known.

## Maintain formula focus

North compares the long form to Abbott and Costello's “Who's on First” and warns that unrelated jokes make audiences lose the scenario [Page 164]. Every line should earn its laugh from the mismatch, not merely occur during it.

## Pair first, justify later

Initial worksheets list assumed/actual people and locations [Pages 164–165]. Dialogue worksheets then create what the mistaken speaker says and how the actual counterpart responds [Pages 165–170]. A doctor/date pairing makes symptoms and requests for ointment sound like romantic disclosure; a dressing-room/confessional pairing turns a request for larger jeans into spiritual language [Pages 165, 169]. Only after useful beats exist should the writer explain the error.

# Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

1. Generate assumed/actual pairs.
2. Select pairs with several overlapping actions or phrases.
3. Write the mistaken speaker's sequence.
4. Write responses plausible in the actual world but misread in the assumed one.
5. Decide between open extended scene and concealed final reveal.
6. Add the shortest workable justification [Pages 164–170].

# Takeaways

The productive unit is not the mistake itself but a line that both worlds interpret differently. Build the dialogue before explaining how the character got there.
