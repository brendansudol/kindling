## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A humor writer's central practical problem is not merely producing funny work but securing readers, which requires making prose accessible and borrowing attention-generating strengths from other media.

Dikkers argues that excellent prose has no value as a professional practice unless an audience encounters it. Because reading competes with more popular and less effortful entertainment, writers must take responsibility for making their work easy to find, understand, and consume. Brevity is the first tactic; visual presentation, video, audio, performance, character voice, and unexpected public placement offer others. Within prose, timing becomes a matter of release, pace, order, typography, and partitioning rather than live delivery. [Page 24] [Page 26] [Page 27] [Page 31] [Page 35] [Page 36]

Essential points:

- Audience discovery is treated as part of the writing problem, not a task a publisher or platform will solve automatically. [Page 24] [Page 25] [Page 26]
- Accessibility means broad availability, clarity, simplicity, and subject matter understandable or interesting to many readers. [Page 27]
- Writers should borrow effective traits from other media without stealing jokes or plagiarizing. [Page 28]
- Prose's main strengths are undivided attention, intimacy, and durability; its weaknesses are low popularity and an impersonal, effortful appearance. [Page 29] [Page 31]
- Prose timing is indirect but real: writers can control release, pace, joke order, visual hierarchy, paragraphing, and other partitions. [Page 35] [Page 36]

## Detailed Walkthrough

### The audience problem precedes professional success

The printed opener separates the chapter's problem from the idea-generation and motivation issues addressed in Chapter 2. Even if a writer creates excellent pages, only a mother or friend may read them. Dikkers insists that finding an audience is not safely delegated to marketing, publishing, or an online platform; it is within the writer's responsibility and must shape the work itself. [Page 24]

He dramatizes the competition through the volume of online writing, the limited attention available inside magazines, and the rarity of serious publisher promotion. He also asserts that most people prefer easier entertainment and that book-sales figures are tiny beside movie or television audiences. These comparisons establish his urgency, but they are authorial claims within the chapter rather than externally verified market analysis. [Page 25] [Page 26]

The practical conclusion is that publication, search optimization, and promotion cannot force reading. The writer must design for attention and make the difficulty of attracting readers an opportunity to improve the work. [Page 25] [Page 26]

### Accessibility and brevity

“Make It Accessible” becomes the chapter's governing tip. Accessible writing is easy to find and read, distributed widely, expressed clearly and simply, and built around subject matter with broad relevance or interest. Dikkers argues that readers already experience reading as work, so prose should not impose needless effort in discovery or comprehension. [Page 27]

His *Garfield* example isolates brevity as the first tactic. Though he says he disliked the strip, its economy caused him to read it almost involuntarily while scanning the comics page. The example is an anecdote, but its function is clear: shortness reduces the threshold between noticing and completing a piece. [Page 27]

### Borrowing rather than plagiarizing

Dikkers provocatively tells writers to “steal,” then carefully limits the term. They must not copy jokes or prose; instead, they should appropriate the attention-getting traits of other humor media. The flower attracting pollinating bees becomes his analogy for presenting writing in forms that attract readers. [Page 28]

He identifies seven humor media: prose, TV/web video, movies, audio/podcast, stage, visual, and street art. The taxonomy is less an exhaustive media theory than a comparative tool for identifying strengths that prose can borrow. [Page 28]

### Prose's distinctive tradeoffs

Prose gives the writer a reader's undivided attention: reading cannot easily continue alongside conversation or chores. It is also intimate because the reader participates in constructing the “word picture,” and durable because old writing can remain consumable without the production-quality barriers Dikkers associates with aging audiovisual work. He especially connects durability and universality to Satire. [Page 29] [Page 31]

Those advantages do not erase prose's small potential audience or its appearance as impersonal, intellectual labor. The chapter's response is not to abandon prose but to package it using traits that reduce those weaknesses. [Page 31]

### What prose can borrow from the other media

The visual medium contributes images. Comic strips and memes combine brief text with pictures, and Dikkers presents Matt Groening's *Life in Hell* as a case where a writer's simple drawings increased accessibility and opened later opportunities. [Page 31]

TV/web video contributes ubiquity, movement, music, and distribution, though full production can be expensive and labor-intensive. A words-only video can still deploy those strengths cheaply; Dikkers cites *The Onion*'s early web-video promotion, whose on-screen headlines and crawls brought readers back to its prose. [Page 31] [Page 32]

Audio/podcasts contribute portability and intimacy and can either promote prose or become a market for scripts. Stage contributes the human connection most intensely. A prose writer can imitate that vitality by creating a convincing “facade”—a personality or intelligence behind the literal words—rather than writing every opinion directly “on the nose.” The chapter points forward to the Character material in Chapter 6 for the method. [Page 32]

Street art contributes surprise and discovery. Dikkers recalls *The Onion*'s free street distribution, where absurd headlines appeared among serious newspapers, and Carol Kolb's parody missing-person flyer. These cases illustrate how an unexpected context can break through media clutter, but the latter is an edgy anecdote rather than evidence that a single tactic generalizes to all writers. [Page 33] [Page 35]

### Timing on the page

The final movement addresses an apparent contradiction: timing is central to comedy, yet prose writers do not control exactly when, how fast, or in what order people read. Dikkers distinguishes performance timing, which he says requires long practice, from prose timing, which has teachable best practices. [Page 35]

The foremost rule is brevity. Removing even a word or two can strengthen a line, and shorter, simpler words help both pacing and accessibility. The author then identifies smaller timing controls: publication time, overall pace, order of jokes, type size, paragraph breaks, bullet points, captions, pull quotes, headlines, and other devices that partition material. [Page 35] [Page 36]

These controls cannot compel every reader to follow the intended route, but they provide guidance and communicate professionalism. Dikkers connects them to later tips such as putting the funny part last, while deferring a fuller account of long-form context to the next book in the series. [Page 36]

The action steps ask readers to compare prose with the other six media, identify borrowed strengths that could attract readers, generate ten imaginative distribution ideas, and write ten one-liners of roughly five to ten words. [Page 36] [Page 37]

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Accessibility through medium transfer

1. Identify prose's weaknesses for the intended audience. [Page 29] [Page 31]
2. Shorten and simplify the work so the entry cost is low. [Page 27] [Page 35]
3. Compare the six other media for a relevant strength—image, motion, sound, personhood, portability, or surprise. [Page 28] [Page 31] [Page 32] [Page 33]
4. Borrow that trait through presentation or distribution without copying anyone's content. [Page 28]
5. Use page-level timing devices to guide the reader's experience. [Page 35] [Page 36]

This five-stage formulation is an analytical reconstruction; the component recommendations are explicit.

## Practical Applications

- Audit a humor piece for how easily a stranger can find it, understand it, and finish it. [Page 27]
- Cut unnecessary words and prefer shorter, simpler alternatives when meaning survives. [Page 35]
- List the strengths and weaknesses of prose against the other six media, then select one borrowed trait suited to the piece. [Page 36] [Page 37]
- Generate multiple playful distribution ideas rather than treating publication as the end of audience development. [Page 37]
- Use paragraphing, typography, captions, headlines, bullets, and joke order to suggest pace and sequence. [Page 35] [Page 36]
- Write ten very short jokes as practice in both accessibility and prose timing. [Page 37]

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- The chapter's claims about reading rates, bestseller revenue, and the rapid growth of online material are rhetorical and uncited in the supplied text. [Page 25] [Page 26]
- Accessibility does not mean using clichés. Dikkers wants broad comprehensibility, while later chapters require original execution. [Page 27]
- Borrowing another medium's traits is explicitly distinguished from stealing jokes or plagiarizing. [Page 28]
- Prose timing is partial control, not exact control; readers may ignore the writer's visual and sequential cues. [Page 36]
- Some statements are culturally and technologically time-bound, including the characterization of radio drama as dead and references to contemporary production formats. [Page 29]

## Takeaways

Humor does not become accessible merely by being published. Writers must reduce friction, present a clear and concise concept, borrow useful formal strengths from other media, and guide attention through prose timing. The point most likely to be misunderstood is the instruction to “steal”: Dikkers means steal presentation traits, never content. [Page 27] [Page 28]
