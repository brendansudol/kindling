## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** A writer turns non-comic Subtext into a discoverable joke by veiling it through one or more of 11 recurring transformations called Funny Filters.

The chapter is the book's central toolbox. Dikkers claims that Irony, Character, Shock, Hyperbole, Wordplay, Reference, Madcap, Parody, Analogy, Misplaced Focus, and Metahumor account for the repeatable ways professional comedy creates laughs. Each filter changes the relation between literal wording and hidden message: it may reverse the message, embody it in a simplified character, violate decorum, exaggerate beyond possibility, play with language, invoke shared experience, introduce absurd physicality, mimic a form, map one domain onto another, stare at the wrong detail, or turn humor itself into the subject. The filters overlap and do not constitute a scientific periodic table; their value is practical diagnosis, generation, and revision. [Page 64] [Page 67] [Page 96]

Essential points:

- The working formula is Subtext filtered through one or more tools to produce a joke and the reader's discovery. [Page 64]
- Filters work best in combinations, but every addition should preserve clarity, one central comic concept, and the intended Subtext. [Page 71] [Page 87] [Page 96]
- Irony, Character, and Reference can organize substantial material; Shock, Wordplay, and Madcap often work best as garnishes unless supported by strong Subtext. [Page 68] [Page 69] [Page 76] [Page 87] [Page 89] [Page 96]
- Ethical and tonal control matters: target deserving power, avoid stereotypes, moderate Shock, and play the premise straight. [Page 67] [Page 72] [Page 76] [Page 90]
- The final criterion overrides the taxonomy: “If they laugh, it's funny.” The tools support judgment but should not paralyze the writer. [Page 112]

## Detailed Walkthrough

### From Subtext to filtered joke

The printed opener states that a joke's Subtext must be “thinly veiled.” A Funny Filter creates distance between the writer's literal statement and intended message, allowing the reader to connect the clues and experience discovery as laughter. Dikkers concedes that other circumstances can cause laughter, but calls those occurrences less reliable; he presents the 11 filters as the professional writer's repeatable options. [Page 64]

That is a strong universality claim, but the chapter later softens the taxonomy. Filter boundaries blur, some tools are subsets or relatives of others, and the list is not intended as a scientific “Periodic Table.” Its purpose is to help a writer create, identify, and diagnose effects, especially while working in Editor mode. [Page 96]

### 1. Irony: assert the polar opposite

Irony expresses the opposite of the intended Subtext. The contrast must be heightened to true polar extremes; the writer plays a game of adopting the contrary position as fully as possible. Dikkers's “nuns are weird” illustration becomes literal praise of their sanity and normality, while *Onion* headlines pair incompatible positions such as an alcoholic father disapproving of a pot-using son. [Page 67]

Irony is not merely an unexpected turn or “Ol' Switcheroo,” because difference is not necessarily opposition. It is also not Sarcasm, which Dikkers calls “Irony light”: the sarcastic speaker's annoyed attitude exposes the real view too openly. Satirical Irony works by adopting the opposite with conviction and playing it straight. [Page 67] [Page 68]

The accompanying targeting rule is “Comfort the Afflicted, Afflict the Comfortable.” A joke may address any subject, but ridicule directed at victims appears mean-spirited; entrenched power, authority, and the status quo are safer and stronger targets. [Page 67]

### 2. Character: simplify a flaw and make it act

Character is the chapter's most performance-proven filter. A comedic character has one to three clear traits and produces jokes by acting in accordance with them. Unlike a dramatic character, it is not designed as a fully realistic, contradictory biography; it symbolizes a shared human weakness that audiences recognize in themselves. [Page 68] [Page 69]

The distinction is functional rather than absolute. Dramatic stories can contain comic-relief characters, and even a dramatic lead can enter a comic moment when one or two simpler traits become dominant. Dikkers's *Kramer vs. Kramer* example reduces a serious character in one scene to being naked and embarrassed, while Irony and Shock reinforce the effect. [Page 69] [Page 71]

Stand-up personas demonstrate the economy: a performer establishes a few traits and repeatedly acts on them. Dikkers names Bill Burr as a psycho who makes sense, Marc Maron as an anxious over-sharer, and David Spade as a Know-It-All. “Heighten Contrast” is the associated craft rule: exaggerate differences between traits, characters, or a comic premise and reality. [Page 71]

The chapter then catalogs durable Archetypes such as the Dummy, Slob, Snob, Know-It-All, Everyperson, Grown-up Child, Klutz, Lothario, Nerd, Robot, Naif, Bumbling Authority, and Trickster. Archetypes are universal patterns; stereotypes attach derogatory traits to racial, national, or oppressed groups and should be avoided except when the work is clearly deconstructing the stereotype or its user. [Page 71] [Page 72]

Because an Archetype can itself feel stale, originality comes either from inventing a fresh one-to-three-trait combination or reinventing an Archetype through an unfamiliar job, identity/species, or environment. The Page 73 OCR corrupts the accented letters in “clichés,” but the canonical note confirms the intended word and the surrounding meaning is clear. [Page 73]

### 3. Shock: violate decorum in proportion to meaning

Shock includes sex, swearing, violence, gross-out material, and other subjects inappropriate for polite mixed company. It startles readers out of social constraint and can range from mild seasoning to overwhelming content. [Page 74] [Page 76]

Dikkers's governing rule is moderation. A little Shock broadens and intensifies a joke; too much narrows the audience toward those who enjoy transgression for its own sake. The more extreme the material, the stronger its Subtext must be. He treats the naked Supreme Court in *America: The Book* as a successful case because the physical shock supports the judgment that justices and laws are fallible. [Page 76]

The pooping-and-farting discussion distinguishes automatic response from sustained craft. The human butt may generate an easy laugh, but repeated bodily-function jokes without Subtext quickly exhaust it. Fart humor is compared to an H-bomb: powerful enough to make what follows seem weaker, so professionals who use it should do so inventively rather than merely making the sound. [Page 76] [Page 77] [Page 78]

“Edgy” comedy is not synonymous with adding more Shock. Dikkers proposes three routes: moderate Shock plus astute Subtext; reducing the time between tragedy and comedy; or appearing to target the afflicted while actually ensuring that the comfortable remains the target. The third is explicitly risky because a failed illusion will look cruel rather than daring. [Page 78]

### 4. Hyperbole: exceed science or reason

Hyperbole is not mild embellishment. The exaggeration must reach an impossible extreme that violates science or reason. Dikkers uses Rodney Dangerfield's lethal bathtub toys and Joan Rivers's target being “two best friends” to show that impossibility, not merely size, drives the device. [Page 80] [Page 81]

The direction can be large or small, positive or negative; what matters is exaggerating the Subtext until reality breaks. One-liners about a martini olive in a urine sample, armed teachers, or politicians keeping their hands in their own pockets illustrate how Hyperbole often layers with Character, Irony, Shock, or Metahumor. [Page 82] [Page 83]

Hyperbole feels especially like deliberate joke writing because it begins from a flat opinion and pushes it to a comic limit. Yet familiar Borscht Belt formulations can themselves become clichés, so extremity does not remove the obligation to find a fresh structure. [Page 81] [Page 83]

### 5. Wordplay: make language do more than its ordinary job

Wordplay includes multiple meanings, invented terms, puns, switches, repetition, sound patterns, anagrams, spoonerisms, oxymorons, tongue twisters, rhymes, and other departures from ordinary verbal function. Funny-sounding words can also contribute a Madcap edge. [Page 83] [Page 84]

Some forms are unfashionable, overused, or difficult to connect to Subtext. Dikkers advises avoiding devices such as alliteration, acronyms, pangrams, mnemonics, Tom Swifties, and typewriter words in Satire unless the joke is deconstructing them. His “Use Verisimilitude” sidebar introduces a wider rule: when copying a speech pattern, character, or format, reproduce it precisely and do not break voice. [Page 85] [Page 86]

The main danger is the groaner—a pun whose entire hidden message is only that one word has two meanings. Better Wordplay makes all meanings function in the sentence, layers devices without becoming confusing, and carries worthwhile Subtext. *The Onion*'s invented snack-nation names work through overwhelming repetition, but also support judgments about distant conflict and American snack culture. [Page 87]

Wordplay often belongs in a title, headline, or individual joke rather than as the architecture of a long piece. Like Shock, it commonly works as garnish. It should be delivered straight because visible striving for cleverness creates a wink that exposes the mechanism. [Page 86] [Page 87] [Page 89]

### 6. Reference: reveal a shared experience freshly

Reference is a relatable observation that makes readers recognize an experience they did not realize was shared. Dikkers calls it perhaps the most useful filter because it underlies many other tools and much Subtext. Its effect is relational: recognition bonds reader and writer. [Page 89]

The scale runs from narrow to broad. Grade-D References are room jokes, in-jokes, and local jokes whose audience already shares special knowledge. Grade-C callbacks point to an earlier joke in the same performance or text and often escalate it. Grade-B References use current events, holidays, weather, or recent speech patterns. Grade-A References identify a fresh, broadly human observation, especially an unnoticed weakness or condition. [Page 90] [Page 91] [Page 93]

The ideal is both recognizable and newly articulated. Jerry Seinfeld's missing sock and George Carlin's contrast between slower “idiots” and faster “maniacs” illustrate everyday experiences reframed as shared insight. Dikkers suggests mentally prompting oneself with “Did you ever notice...?” but warns not to use that now-clichéd wording on the page. [Page 89] [Page 93] [Page 94]

Reference creates an accessibility tension: the more specialized the shared knowledge, the smaller the audience. A writer should therefore distinguish intentionally local work from material that needs broader reach. [Page 90]

### 7. Madcap: use physical or verbal absurdity with purpose

Madcap translates physical comedy into prose through slapstick descriptions, pratfalls, funny objects and words, non sequiturs, and cartoonish behavior. The effect appears random to readers but should be purposeful to the writer, with Subtext beneath the silliness. [Page 94]

Without Subtext, sustained Madcap becomes Kid Stuff. With Subtext, a silly detail can symbolize the judgment itself. Dikkers reads *The Onion*'s Secretary of Agriculture meeting a cabbage as expressing indifference to government activity or the absurdity of bureaucracy. Monty Python's “Ministry of Silly Walks” similarly turns lunatic movement into a judgment about government administration. [Page 96] [Page 97]

Madcap is often best as a pinch that makes an intellectual joke more physically accessible, as Kramer does within *Seinfeld*. It can also carry a whole piece when the silliness consistently serves the same message. Familiar props—banana peels, rubber chickens, Groucho glasses—are clichés unless the joke targets those conventions themselves. ### 8. Parody: copy form, replace content

Parody mocks an entertainment or information product, creator, format, or medium. It depends on audience familiarity: a target few readers know cannot contribute much immediate recognition. [Page 99]

Presentation itself can produce much of the laugh. A recognizable set, design, voice, or format primes an audience before substantive words begin. *The Onion*'s news style, *Diary of a Wimpy Kid*'s diary form, and Ian Frazier's legal-brief parody “Coyote v. Acme” illustrate different targets and scales. [Page 100] [Page 101]

Parody can carry two hidden judgments: the piece's core Subtext and an opinion about the mimicked form. An *Onion* government story may say government is incompetent while its news parody simultaneously questions journalism. Frazier can expose both cartoon logic and legalistic culture. [Page 101]

The one governing rule is verisimilitude. Copy form, format, voice, and presentation as closely as possible while supplying new content. Half-accurate imitation weakens recognition; precise imitation played straight can approach a hoax and produce an additional satisfaction when some readers mistake it for the real thing. [Page 102]

The chapter pairs Parody with two editorial cautions: do not become so attached to an idea that feedback cannot change it, and use specific details—especially near the funny ending—rather than broad description. [Page 101] [Page 103]

### 9. Analogy: expose one domain and conceal the other

Analogy compares two disparate things and makes the similarities visible. In Dikkers's comic version, only one side should remain overt; the second is the “hidden secret” that readers infer from clues, separate from the deeper Subtext. [Page 104]

His model is *Animal Farm*: farm animals are overt, the Russian Revolution is concealed, and “power corrupts” is the additional Subtext. Readers unfamiliar with the historical mapping can still enjoy the overt story, which Dikkers presents as an accessibility advantage. [Page 104] [Page 105]

The method is “mapping.” Once the hidden side is established or implied, every recognizable correspondence between the overt and concealed domains creates another joke beat. Steve Martin's smoking/farting bit, for example, makes smoking the hidden structure and maps airplane sections, quitting, weight gain, and post-sex habits onto farting. [Page 108]

Good Analogy therefore requires strong distance between the domains, concealment of one side, and numerous clear correspondences. Naming the hidden side repeatedly ruins the reader's discovery. [Page 104] [Page 105] [Page 108]

### 10. Misplaced Focus: notice everything except the main issue

Misplaced Focus directs intense attention to a lesser, tangential matter while pretending not to see the important one. The omission brings the real issue—and often the Subtext—more forcefully into the reader's mind. [Page 108] [Page 109]

The tool is suited to subjects that already provoke strong concern because the writer's false blindness creates righteous indignation. Dikkers reads Swift's “A Modest Proposal” as focusing on eating poor children rather than on their poverty, forcing readers to confront the social failure that makes the grotesque proposal conceivable. [Page 109]

As with Analogy, Misplaced Focus may contain a second hidden item: an “Elephant in the Room” related to but not identical with the Subtext. *The Onion*'s secondhand-coolness story pretends to care about smoking's appearance rather than health, while a line about a dead teenager's mother lacking phone-update help foregrounds a trivial inconvenience to expose the ignored loss. [Page 109] [Page 110]

### 11. Metahumor: make humor itself the target

Metahumor steps back from a joke to mock humor, its effects, its media, its practitioners, or the human fact of laughter. Because the subject can be insular, writers must make it accessible beyond comedy professionals. [Page 110] [Page 111]

Dikkers orders four uses by difficulty. Type A examines the concept of humor itself and can be revelatory but esoteric. Type B deconstructs comedy with detached, low-emotion analysis—the Robot Archetype applied to laughter. Type C criticizes respected comic work or current conventions and therefore requires confidence and a deserving target. Type D mocks obviously weak comedy or exhausted clichés, making it the easiest but least daring target. [Page 111] [Page 112] [Page 115]

Metahumor also salvages failure. A performer can acknowledge a bad joke and invite an audience to laugh at the failure, using self-deprecation and shared discomfort. Steve Martin's “hilarious comedy jokes” and the *Finding Nemo* clownfish who cannot tell jokes layer meta-awareness with Character and Irony. [Page 115]

Antihumor is a subset in which an expected joke is replaced by an obvious lack of humor. It can generate cult enthusiasm but also anger audiences who feel betrayed, so Dikkers recommends caution. Andy Kaufman reading *The Great Gatsby* on stage is his principal illustration. [Page 115] [Page 116]

The chapter encourages writers to notice their filter preferences while practicing weaker ones. Using multiple filters can reach readers with different tastes, but this breadth should not become mechanical clutter. [Page 115]

### Cross-cutting editorial rules and the final standard

The filters repeatedly depend on a small set of controls:

- Heighten the central contrast rather than settling for a weak difference. [Page 71]
- Direct ridicule toward power or otherwise deserving targets. [Page 67]
- Avoid stereotypes and stale comic symbols unless the joke is clearly examining them. [Page 72] [Page 98]
- Use Shock and Madcap proportionally and in service of Subtext. [Page 76] [Page 96]
- Maintain verisimilitude, consistency, and a straight delivery without winking. [Page 85] [Page 90] [Page 102]
- Preserve specificity, concision, and the reader's work of discovery. [Page 87] [Page 103] [Page 104]
- Stay willing to cut a cherished idea when evidence says it does not work. [Page 101]

Dikkers finally reduces the rules to one outcome: if the audience laughs, the material is funny. The taxonomy should reduce uncertainty and improve craft, not overwhelm the writer or replace reader response. [Page 112]

The chapter's action step is an extended drill: over successive days, write ten one-liners, titles, or jokes with each filter, practicing all 11 rather than remaining inside one's natural preferences. The transcript says “next 11 chapters” although the context clearly describes the 11 filters and ten days, an internal wording inconsistency that should not be silently repaired. [Page 117]

## Practical Applications

- Write the intended Subtext as a flat sentence, then attempt a version through each filter rather than assuming the first tool is the best fit. [Page 64]
- Combine filters when each adds a distinct layer, but retain one intelligible concept and message. [Page 71] [Page 87] [Page 96]
- For Character, specify no more than three traits and draft actions that display rather than merely label them. [Page 69]
- For Shock or Madcap, remove any element that does not strengthen Subtext or accessibility. [Page 76] [Page 96]
- For Reference, identify exactly what experience the intended audience must share and whether that audience is broad enough. [Page 90] [Page 91] [Page 93]
- For Parody, create a checklist of the target's voice, format, design, syntax, and conventions, then reproduce those formal traits consistently. [Page 85] [Page 102]
- For Analogy, keep a two-column private map but expose only one column in the final joke. **Interpretation:** The two-column device is an application of Dikkers's explicit mapping method, not a worksheet he prescribes. [Page 104] [Page 108]
- Practice all filters, including the uncomfortable ones, to broaden both skill and potential audience. [Page 115] [Page 117]

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- The claim that only these 11 filters produce repeatable professional laughter is asserted rather than empirically demonstrated in the chapter. [Page 64] [Page 67]
- Dikkers explicitly calls the definitions loose and overlapping, which limits rigid classification. Parody is closely related to Reference, and funny words can belong to both Wordplay and Madcap. [Page 96]
- Archetypes are not licenses for stereotypes; the distinction depends on universality, target, and treatment. [Page 72]
- Ethical guidance is framed through target direction, but a joke that intends to “afflict the comfortable” may still be perceived differently by audiences. The chapter recognizes this risk most clearly in edgy comedy. [Page 67] [Page 78]
- Examples often receive Dikkers's own Subtext readings. Those readings model analysis but do not eliminate alternate plausible interpretations. [Page 87] [Page 96]
- Antihumor can work precisely by violating expectations, which makes audience rejection a foreseeable cost rather than definitive evidence of technical error. [Page 116]
- The final rule about laughter places empirical audience response above theoretical compliance; a technically classifiable joke can still fail. [Page 112]

## Takeaways

The 11 filters are best understood as lenses, not laws. Their value lies in forcing different relations between message and wording, giving the writer alternatives when a joke is flat and vocabulary for explaining why a draft works or fails. The most easily missed point is that a filter by itself is rarely enough: recognizable technique without Subtext becomes a groaner, gratuitous shock, empty silliness, or a narrow in-joke. The highest-leverage practice is to generate across the whole set, then let audience response and disciplined editing decide what survives. [Page 76] [Page 87] [Page 90] [Page 96] [Page 112]
