## Chapter in Brief

**Central thesis:** Surprise is the indispensable condition of humor, so prose writers must eliminate comedy clichés and develop fresh work toward the layered, broadly accessible category Dikkers calls Satire.

The chapter distinguishes crafted prose laughter from social and accidental laughter, then claims that all humor depends on surprise even though not every surprise is funny. That principle makes cliché-busting a primary editorial task. Dikkers places written humor on a five-category spectrum—Kid Stuff, Conversational, Snark, Formulaic Humor, and Satire—ordered by increasing layering and sophistication. Formulaic Humor skillfully uses comic tools; Satire adds a hidden, meaningful element developed in the next chapter. For longer works, however, dramatic story structure must carry audience interest while jokes become secondary. [Page 40] [Page 41] [Page 42] [Page 45] [Page 46] [Page 50] [Page 52]

Essential points:

- Dikkers asserts that crafted prose laughter is exceptionally rare and unsupported by performance cues or group response. [Page 40] [Page 41]
- Surprise is necessary but not sufficient: frightening, annoying, and merely shocking surprises need not be funny. [Page 41]
- Comedy clichés include not only phrases but exhausted jokes, topics, characters, and structural patterns. [Page 42] [Page 44]
- The prose-humor spectrum describes different degrees of layering and audience breadth, not merely different subjects. [Page 46] [Page 47] [Page 50] [Page 52]
- Pure comedic escalation cannot, in Dikkers's account, sustain long stories; long-form work requires dramatic structure. [Page 52]

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Isolating crafted prose laughter

The printed opener praises humor's personal and social value and cites health, friendship, income, and longevity claims. These claims motivate the craft but are not documented in the visible chapter and should be treated as authorial framing, not verified findings. [Page 38]

Dikkers then divides laughter into nervous social laughter, spontaneous laughter at real-life events, crafted performance laughter, and an extremely small remainder caused only by words. He assigns dramatic percentages—more than 90 percent social anxiety, roughly 9 percent spontaneous events, less than 1 percent crafted comedy, and about .00001 percent crafted prose comedy. The figures are presented through a chart and prose but without methodology in the captured text. Their analytical function is to emphasize how rare unassisted written laughter is. [Page 40] [Page 41]

Performance laughter can be amplified by a social contract, crowd behavior, audience warm-up, and recorded or canned laughter. Prose lacks these supports; the chapter therefore seeks techniques that produce a strong involuntary response from words alone. [Page 41]

### Surprise as the necessary ingredient

Dikkers separates helpful comic qualities—relatability, truth, timing, tragedy, taboo-breaking—from the one element he considers required: surprise. Even a predictable event must unfold in a surprising way to become funny. He immediately qualifies the formula: some surprises frighten, shock, or annoy, so the book concerns particular varieties of surprise that generate laughter. [Page 41]

This claim advances the cumulative argument. Chapter 3 described attention-getting surprise in presentation; Chapter 4 makes surprise internal to the joke itself.

### Cliché-busting

Because clichés are already known, they cannot supply the necessary freshness. Dikkers begins with stale phrases but broadens the humor-writer's definition to recycled jokes, overworked subjects, stock actions, and familiar patterns. His examples range from airline food and “That's what she said” to racialized speech comparisons and a character insulting someone who is standing behind them. [Page 42] [Page 44]

The editorial test is direct: ask whether every aspect of the writing is original. Dikkers treats reliance on clichés as a professional diagnostic because sophisticated readers recognize them immediately and may stop reading. He acknowledges that an old joke can surprise someone who has never heard it, but argues that targeting only such readers narrows rather than enlarges accessibility; original tools can entertain both less and more experienced audiences. [Page 44] [Page 45]

### The five categories of prose humor

Dikkers presents a spectrum from basic and narrowly appealing to layered and broadly appealing: Kid Stuff, Conversational, Snark, Formulaic Humor, and Satire. The Page 46 diagram places examples along that spectrum, but the review notes that the hand-drawn spacing and overlapping brackets were transcribed only approximately. The reliable evidence is the category order and general movement toward sophistication. [Page 46]

**Kid Stuff** uses basic incongruity, simple puns, social-rule violations, gross-out, and Madcap effects. It appeals especially to young children, though Dikkers says it contains the seeds of more advanced humor. [Page 47]

**Conversational humor** presents the author's real personality and opinions directly, without a constructed comic voice. **Snark** adds a knowing, sarcastic persona, but often exposes the writer beneath and relies heavily on fashionable verbal clichés. Dikkers allows that both can be skillful—he names Dave Barry, David Sedaris, Mike Nelson, John Hodgman, Sarah Vowell, and *Spy*—but argues that imitative Snark is overused and hard to distinguish. [Page 47] [Page 49]

At this point he inserts “Proofread.” Correct spelling, grammar, and syntax do not create humor, but errors can cause otherwise comparable work to lose professional opportunities. [Page 49]

**Formulaic Humor** deploys multiple layers and the professional tools later named as the 11 Funny Filters. It often uses characters, formats, and facades rather than the writer's first-person voice, thereby placing productive distance between author and audience. Dikkers associates it with professional comedy in prose, television, and film. [Page 50]

**Satire** uses the tools below it, especially Formulaic Humor, but adds a hidden feature that makes laughter more satisfying and memorable. The chapter deliberately withholds that “secret ingredient” until Chapter 5. Dikkers argues that Satire can incorporate accessible low-level effects while also rewarding sophisticated readers, giving it the broadest potential appeal. [Page 52]

### Comedic structure and dramatic structure

A boxed discussion distinguishes a short comedic structure—establishing and escalating one joke—from dramatic story structure. Dikkers says the former works for one-liners, short articles, and sketches of roughly five to ten minutes but cannot sustain a novel, screenplay, or other long narrative. In longer work, story becomes primary and laughter secondary; even highly comic films need emotional stakes and plot. [Page 52]

The chapter does not teach dramatic structure. It recommends first becoming competent with the comedy tools so they can later be infused into longer stories. This is an important scope limit: the manual teaches joke construction and short comic structures, not a complete theory of comic narrative. [Page 52]

The action steps ask readers to identify their desired kind of laughter and current category, examine their cliché habits, and write ten one-sentence jokes without clichés. [Page 53]

## Practical Applications

- Audit a draft for familiar jokes, exhausted topics, stock characters, and predictable comic structures; replace them with a fresh angle. [Page 42] [Page 44]
- Decide what kind of laughter and what category of prose humor the work is trying to produce. [Page 46] [Page 53]
- Use lower-spectrum devices when useful, but layer them with more sophisticated tools rather than assuming refinement requires abandoning simple humor. [Page 52]
- Proofread submissions because craft errors can obscure or disqualify strong ideas. [Page 49]
- For a short piece, establish and escalate one concept; for a long work, do not rely on that comic escalation to replace story. [Page 52]
- Practice with ten one-sentence jokes that avoid known clichés. [Page 53]

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

- The numerical breakdown of laughter is asserted without visible sourcing or methodology. It should not be treated as a verified measurement. [Page 40] [Page 41]
- Surprise is necessary in Dikkers's model but explicitly not sufficient; a surprise can produce fear or annoyance instead. [Page 41]
- Accessibility and sophistication are not opposites. Dikkers argues that Satire can use simple devices while adding layers for more experienced readers. [Page 45] [Page 52]
- The category labels mix voice, technique, audience, and evaluative judgment; they are a working editorial spectrum rather than mutually exclusive formal genres.
- The hard limit placed on comedic structure in long works is a strong authorial rule. The chapter offers examples but not a systematic survey of counterexamples. [Page 52]
- The chart on Page 46 is OCR-approximate in spatial detail, so no interpretation here depends on exact placements of named writers and publications.

## Connections

The chapter turns Chapter 3's accessibility goal inward: broad appeal now requires fresh surprise, not merely easy presentation. Formulaic Humor previews Chapter 6's tools, while Satire's undisclosed extra layer sets up Chapter 5's account of Subtext. [Page 45] [Page 50] [Page 52]

## Takeaways

The chapter's governing editorial question is whether the reader can predict the move. Clichés fail because the surprise is gone; more developed humor layers original technique and meaning. Yet sophisticated does not mean obscure: Dikkers's ideal Satire remains accessible across the spectrum. The main scope warning is that a strong joke method does not substitute for story structure in longer work. [Page 45] [Page 52]
