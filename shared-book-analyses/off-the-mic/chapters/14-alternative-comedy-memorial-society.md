## Chapter in Brief

**Organizing purpose:** John-Luke Roberts documents how the Alternative Comedy Memorial Society (ACMS) used ritual, rules, overcrowding, and shared participation to create a raucous but respectful room where experiments and failures could be valuable in themselves.

ACMS was founded by Roberts and Thom Tuck to fill a perceived gap for genuine one-offs and “stupidest ideas,” drawing on memories of earlier alternative nights [Location 3249]. Its recurring devices reclassified failure as noble, limited heckling to supportive absurdities, destabilized conventional audience expectation, created a semi-democratic board of performers, and ended with a deliberately excessive group ritual [Locations 3249–3254]. The night worked because the audience and acts learned the local contract; material that succeeded there often failed elsewhere [Locations 3268–3282].

Essential points:

- A room can make experimentation safer by changing the audience’s role, not merely telling performers to be braver [Locations 3249–3254].
- Ritual language makes the audience publicly complicit in accepting failure [Location 3249].
- Constraints on heckling can preserve noisy participation while redirecting hostility into the show’s tone [Location 3254].
- Success is local to a performance ecology; ACMS-specific work did not automatically transfer to other venues [Location 3268].
- A creative institution can end or become intermittent after accomplishing its purpose rather than seeking permanent expansion [Locations 3268–3282].

## Detailed Walkthrough

### Establish the contract through comic rules

The founders wanted a space where failure was celebrated and the audience could be loud without becoming disrespectful. Their call-and-response—hosts call “A Failure,” listeners answer “A Noble Failure”—turned tolerance into an enacted identity. The joke about legal complicity captures the serious mechanism: people who publicly participate in the norm are more likely to sustain it [Location 3249].

Other devices continuously disrupt ordinary club evaluation. A foreign-language opener is deliberately mistranslated as a familiar Ed Byrne routine and accused of theft. Heckles are allowed only from a pre-approved list such as “We appreciate what you’re trying to do.” An overfull bill and then five more acts reject efficient programming. A regular board nominally holds democratic power, while a group performance of *Captain Planet* closes the night [Location 3254]. The rules model the very failure and excess the club claims to value.

### A distinct room produces distinct work

The club began in 2011 and succeeded for both audience and acts. Performers stood among listeners, joining or disrupting as needed. Roberts explicitly notes that pieces worked at ACMS and failed elsewhere, sometimes to the acts’ anger [Location 3268]. The point is not that the other rooms were wrong; the local audience had been trained into a different interpretive and behavioral contract.

ACMS moved through pubs, a barge, a big top, a bar, and theatres. By 2013 it had attracted established experimental performers and a television pilot, while similar nights flourished. Having created the desired space, stopping seemed sensible—though the comic account says they continued, then settled into occasional appearances and a plan for an overlarge final show [Locations 3268–3282]. The organization’s “pleasantly dusky” state preserves the anti-institutional tone instead of declaring either clean success or failure.

## Frameworks, Processes, and Mental Models

### Failure-friendly room design

1. Define the behavior sought: risky one-offs and respectful noise.
2. Give the audience a repeatable ritual that endorses imperfect attempts.
3. Channel disruption through bounded, tonally appropriate rules.
4. Keep performers physically and socially present in the room.
5. Repeat enough shared absurdity for the local contract to become self-reinforcing [Locations 3249–3268].

## Practical Applications

- When asking artists to take risks, redesign audience incentives and participation rules at the same time [Locations 3249–3254].
- Use repeated, humorous rituals to communicate a norm more memorably than a solemn announcement [Location 3249].
- Evaluate an experimental piece inside the room it was designed for before treating transfer failure as proof that the experiment had no value [Location 3268].

## Assumptions, Nuances, and Limitations

Roberts calls this a “snapshot” and writes in the club’s absurd comic voice. It is an insider case, not a controlled assessment of whether each trick caused the atmosphere. The claimed celebration of failure coexists with clear evidence of success—popular shows, prominent performers, and a television pilot—so “failure” is a permission structure and aesthetic value, not indifference to audience engagement [Locations 3249–3282].

## Connections

ACMS institutionalizes the room-design logic dispersed across earlier chapters. Provenza uses prompts to make experts vulnerable; Brigstocke asks for formats where more comic types can flourish; Roberts changes audience behavior itself [Provenza interview, Locations 1089–1154; Brigstocke interview, Locations 1692–1722; ACMS, Locations 3249–3268]. ## Takeaways

Telling performers that failure is acceptable is weaker than building a room that proves it. ACMS’s distinctive contribution is environmental: ritualize generosity, constrain hostility, and make the audience part of the experiment’s safety architecture.
